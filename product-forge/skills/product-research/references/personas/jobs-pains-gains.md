# Jobs, Pains, Gains — the Value Proposition Canvas (Strategyzer)

The Value Proposition Canvas (VPC), from Osterwalder, Pigneur, Bernarda, and Smith's _Value Proposition Design_ (2014), is a tool for designing a value proposition that a specific customer segment actually wants. It zooms in on two of the nine Business Model Canvas blocks — Customer Segments and Value Propositions — and forces you to make the connection between them explicit. As a **persona/research artifact** its job is to keep the description of the customer (the right side) honest and observed, separate from the thing you sell (the left side). It is not a persona template per se; it is the structured profile of a segment's _circumstance_ that a good persona should be built on.

## The two sides: customer profile and value map

The canvas has two halves that must be kept rigorously distinct.

- **The customer profile** (right, a circle) describes a customer segment in the team's mind — observed, not invented. Three parts: **customer jobs**, **pains**, and **gains**.
- **The value map** (left, a square) describes how you intend to create value for that segment — designed, in your control. Three parts: **products and services**, **pain relievers**, and **gain creators**.

The shapes are a deliberate mnemonic: the **square** value map should slot into the **circle** customer profile. You sketch the profile _first_, from evidence, and only then design a map against it. Strategyzer's first and most-cited mistake is treating the canvas as one block instead of two: conflating jobs/pains/gains (what the customer experiences, outside your control) with pain relievers/gain creators (what you build).

## The customer profile (the right side)

- **Customer jobs** — what customers are trying to get done in their work and lives, described in their own words. Three types, and you must reach past the first:
  - **Functional jobs** — a concrete task or problem to solve (write a report, get to work, eat healthily). Most visible, most over-represented in weak canvases.
  - **Social jobs** — how the customer wants to be _perceived_ by others (look competent, look in-the-know).
  - **Emotional jobs** — how the customer wants to _feel_ (secure, in control, unstressed). Jobs also carry **context** (a job done on a phone on a commute is a different job from the same task at a desk) and relative **importance** (some jobs are trivial, some are make-or-break).
- **Pains** — anything that annoys customers before, during, or after trying to get a job done, or that blocks them from getting it done at all. Strategyzer groups them as **undesired outcomes/problems** (it doesn't work, side effects), **obstacles** (no time, no budget, too hard to start), and **risks** (what could go wrong — financial, social, technical). Rate each pain by **severity** (mild ↔ extreme), the mirror of gain importance.
- **Gains** — the outcomes and benefits customers want, including the **required** (without it, the solution doesn't work), **expected** (basic things we assume), **desired** (things we'd love but don't expect), and **unexpected** (delight beyond expectation). Rate each by **relevance** (nice-to-have ↔ essential).

## The value map (the left side)

- **Products and services** — the list of what your value proposition is built on. A list alone creates no value; it only does so in relation to a specific profile.
- **Pain relievers** — explicitly how your products and services kill specific customer **pains**. The best ones address extreme pains, not trivial ones.
- **Gain creators** — explicitly how your products and services produce specific customer **gains**. The best ones produce gains customers care most about.

You don't have to relieve every pain or create every gain — and trying to is Strategyzer's fifth mistake (see below). A few high-relevance relievers and creators, sharply targeted, beat a long undifferentiated list.

## Fit: where the value map meets the profile

**Fit** is the goal of the canvas — the state where your value map addresses the jobs, pains, and gains that the customer segment cares most about. _Value Proposition Design_ frames fit in three escalating stages:

1. **Problem–solution fit** — you have evidence that customers care about certain jobs, pains, and gains, and you've designed a value proposition (on paper) that addresses them. Fit "on paper."
2. **Product–market fit** — you have evidence in the market that your products and services, pain relievers, and gain creators actually create customer value and get traction. Fit "in the market."
3. **Business-model fit** — you have evidence that the value proposition can be embedded in a profitable and scalable business model.

The discipline: fit is **earned with evidence**, never asserted. You sketch a profile and a map as hypotheses, then test them against real customers and adjust. The canvas is a thinking tool, not proof.

## How to build one (the working sequence)

```text
1. Pick ONE customer segment.            One profile per segment — never blend payer + user.
2. Sketch the customer profile FIRST.    Jobs, then pains, then gains — from observation,
                                          not from your product. Set your solution aside.
3. Push past functional jobs.            Force at least one social and one emotional job.
4. Rank.                                  Jobs by importance; pains by severity; gains by relevance.
5. Sketch the value map AGAINST it.       Products/services → pain relievers → gain creators,
                                          each pointed at a specific ranked pain or gain.
6. Check fit.                             Does the map hit the high-ranked items? Drop the rest.
7. Test with real customers.             Move problem–solution fit toward product–market fit;
                                          revise the profile when evidence contradicts it.
```

## Mistakes to avoid (Strategyzer's five, plus persona-specific ones)

Strategyzer publishes five recurring failure modes; the first four are the ones that corrupt a persona/profile most.

1. **Treating the canvas as one block, not two.** Mixing jobs/pains/gains (observed customer reality) with pain relievers/gain creators (your designed response). If a sticky note describes something _you_ do, it belongs on the left; if it describes the customer's world, it belongs on the right. Keep them apart.
2. **Blending several segments into one profile.** Putting the payer's jobs and the end-user's jobs on the same canvas obscures whose needs you're serving. One profile per distinct segment; separate canvases.
3. **Writing the profile through the lens of your solution.** Listing only the jobs/pains/gains your existing product happens to address, so the canvas just flatters what you already built. Fix: take an "anthropological" stance, set your solution aside, and use _Five Whys_ to reach the real motivation.
4. **Only capturing functional jobs.** They're the most visible, so teams stop there and miss the social and emotional jobs where differentiation actually lives.
5. **Trying to address every pain and gain.** Spreading effort across everything yields a mediocre, unfocused proposition. Prioritize the highest-impact jobs, pains, and gains and make deliberate trade-offs.
6. **(Persona-specific) Inventing the profile instead of researching it.** The VPC has no built-in defense against fabrication: a confidently filled canvas can be pure assumption. The profile is only as good as the customer evidence behind it — pair it with interviews / observation (see the goal-directed-personas and persona-antipatterns references in this axis).

## Scoring guide: good vs. bad jobs/pains/gains

Use this to score a Value Proposition Canvas or any persona built on one. A strong profile is **observed, segment-specific, multi-dimensional, and ranked**; a weak one is invented, blended, functional-only, and flat.

| Dimension | Bad (low score) | Good (high score) |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Source** | Filled in a meeting from memory; no customer contact | Built from interviews/observation of the real segment; claims traceable to evidence |
| **Job depth** | Only functional ("send invoices") | Functional + social + emotional, in context ("look on top of my finances to my accountant, feel in control at month-end") |
| **Job phrasing** | Names your product ("use our dashboard") — that's a feature | A customer goal stated independently of any solution |
| **Segment hygiene** | One canvas mixing buyer, user, and influencer | One profile per segment; payer and user separated |
| **Pains/gains** | Vague, unranked, all "important" | Specific, rated by severity/relevance, with a clear top few |
| **Value-map link** | Relievers/creators floating, not tied to any pain or gain | Each reliever kills a named ranked pain; each creator produces a named ranked gain |
| **Fit claim** | "Customers will love this" asserted | Fit framed as a tested hypothesis with a path from problem–solution to product–market fit |

### Worked contrast

```text
BAD  (invented, functional-only, solution-framed)
  Segment: "Small business owners, 30–50"
  Job:     "Use our app to manage money"
  Pain:    "Doesn't have our app"
  Gain:    "Has our app"
  → Circular: the profile is just a mirror of the product. Untestable, un-actionable.

GOOD (observed, multi-dimensional, solution-independent)
  Segment: "Solo consultants who invoice 3–8 clients/month, do their own books"
  Jobs:    functional — "get paid on time without chasing clients"
           social     — "look organized and professional to clients and my accountant"
           emotional  — "stop dreading month-end reconciliation"
  Pains:   "I forget who hasn't paid" (severe); "reconciling takes a full evening" (severe);
           "I'm scared I'll get tax wrong" (extreme — a risk)
  Gains:   required — "an accurate record at year-end";
           desired  — "see at a glance who owes me";
           unexpected — "it nudges late payers for me"
  → Each pain/gain is a target a pain reliever or gain creator can be pointed at and tested.
```

## Relationship to JTBD (a labeled note)

The VPC's "customer jobs" borrow Clayton C.'s Jobs-to-be-Done vocabulary, but the two are not identical and the lineages are sometimes conflated. JTBD (Clayton C.) is a theory of _causality_ — why a customer hires a product to make progress in a circumstance — and is wary of any artifact that re-centers the product. The VPC is a _design canvas_ that operationalizes "jobs" alongside pains and gains and pairs them with a value map. Tony Ulwick's Outcome-Driven Innovation treats jobs and "desired outcomes" far more quantitatively still. Use the VPC for structured profiling; reach for the JTBD references (in `product-methodology`) when you need the underlying theory of demand. (Labeled as a known terminological overlap, not a contradiction.)
