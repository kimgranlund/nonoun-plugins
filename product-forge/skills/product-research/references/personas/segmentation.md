# Segmentation: behavioral / needs-based vs. demographic

Segmentation is how you cut a market into groups so a product and its personas can be aimed at one of them. The choice of _cut_ is the consequential decision: segment on the wrong axis and every downstream persona, value proposition, and roadmap inherits the error. This reference draws the line between **demographic** segmentation (who the customer _is_) and **behavioral / needs-based / jobs-based** segmentation (what the customer is trying to _do_), and says when each is the right tool — because the honest answer is not "always behavioral."

## The four classical bases (and the trap in the first one)

Marketing's textbook segmentation bases (Kotler/Keller) are:

- **Demographic** — age, gender, income, education, occupation, family stage, etc. Cheap, available, and easy to act on in media buying.
- **Geographic** — region, climate, urban/rural, language market.
- **Psychographic** — values, attitudes, lifestyle, personality.
- **Behavioral** — what people actually do: usage rate, occasion, loyalty, benefits sought, buying stage.

The trap is that demographic data is the most _available_, so teams default to it and mistake a description of _who buys_ for an explanation of _why_. Behavioral and needs-based segments are harder to build but cut closer to cause.

## The core critique: demographics correlate, they don't cause

The sharpest statement is Clayton C., Cook, and Hall's "Marketing Malpractice" (HBR, 2005) and the _Competing Against Luck_ line of work. Their claim:

> "The fact that you're 18 to 35 years old with a college degree does not cause you to buy a product. It may be correlated with the decision, but it doesn't cause it."

Clayton C. calls the failure mode **"demographic determinism"** — the belief that knowing the customer's demographic profile predicts what they'll buy. It doesn't, because "consumers usually don't go about their shopping by conforming to particular segments." A purchase is _caused_ by a **job** that arises in a circumstance; demographics are at best a shadow that sometimes correlates with that job.

The milkshake study is the canonical demonstration. A chain segmented its milkshake market two conventional ways — by **product** (the milkshake category) and by **demographics** (a profile of the typical milkshake buyer) — and improving the product against that profile moved nothing. Reframing around the **job** revealed that ~40% of milkshakes sold in the early morning to solo commuters hiring them to make a long, boring drive more bearable (tidy, one-handed, slow to finish). Same demographic could hire the milkshake for a completely different afternoon job. The lesson: **segment by the job, not the buyer.** "Marketing Malpractice" generalizes it — companies that "segment and measure markets by product categories" or by demographics typically find that when they instead segment by job, "their market is much larger… than they had thought."

## Three behavioral / needs-based cuts (and how they differ)

These are not synonyms; they sit on a ladder from observed action toward underlying cause.

| Cut | Segments on | Strength | Limit |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Behavioral** | Observed actions — usage frequency, occasion, feature adoption, loyalty, lifecycle stage | Grounded in real, measurable behavior; great for timing and targeting | Tells you _what_ they did, not _why_; past behavior need not reveal the underlying need |
| **Needs-based** | The problems and desired benefits customers are trying to satisfy | Cuts to motivation; stable across products and time | Harder to observe; requires research, not just analytics |
| **Jobs-based (JTBD)** | The "job" — the progress a customer is trying to make in a circumstance | Names the cause of the purchase; reveals the true competitive set | Defining the job correctly is genuinely hard and contested (see fork below) |

The unifying idea: needs-based and jobs-based cuts are **causal** (they explain choice), behavioral cuts are **observational** (they record choice), and demographic cuts are **descriptive** (they label the chooser). The closer to cause, the more durable the persona built on it — and the more research it takes to produce.

## When each is actually right

Behavioral/needs-based is the default for _product_ decisions, but demographics earn their place in specific, legitimate cases. Pick by the decision you're feeding.

**Use demographic / geographic segmentation when:**

- **The product's value genuinely varies by demographic.** Some needs really do track age (retirement products), life stage (prenatal care), or geography (tax software per jurisdiction, localization). Here demography is close to causal, not a proxy.
- **You're buying media or sizing a market.** Ad platforms and TAM estimates are denominated in demographic and geographic terms; you need them to _reach_ and _count_ people even if you _designed_ for a job.
- **There are regulatory or access constraints** (age gating, regional availability).
- **It's a fast, cheap first cut** before research — explicitly held as a hypothesis, not a finding.

**Use behavioral / needs-based / jobs-based segmentation when:**

- **You're deciding what to build or how to position it.** Product, feature, and value-proposition decisions need the _why_; demographics will mislead you (the milkshake trap).
- **The same demographic shows divergent behavior**, or different demographics show the same behavior — the signal that demography isn't the real axis.
- **You're defining personas for design.** Goal-directed personas are built on goals and behavior, not demographics (see goal-directed-personas in this axis).
- **You have behavioral data or can do the research** to find the underlying need.

The mature practice combines them: **behavioral/needs/jobs to decide what and why; demographics/geography to reach, size, and comply.** They answer different questions — treat them as complementary, not as rivals where one must win.

## How to choose a segmentation axis (working sequence)

```text
1. State the decision the segmentation will feed.   "What do we build?" vs. "Where do we advertise?"
2. If it's a PRODUCT/positioning decision →         segment on jobs/needs/behavior (the WHY).
   If it's a REACH/sizing/compliance decision →      demographic/geographic is fine (the WHO).
3. Pressure-test any demographic cut for causality.  "Does being [demo] CAUSE this purchase,
                                                       or merely correlate?" If correlate → dig for the job.
4. Build segments from evidence.                     Behavioral = analytics; needs/jobs = interviews
                                                       (specific past stories, not opinions).
5. Check the segments are actionable + distinct.     Each segment should imply a DIFFERENT response;
                                                       if two segments get the same product, they're one.
6. Layer for go-to-market.                            Map demographic/geographic descriptors ONTO the
                                                       needs/jobs segments so you can reach them.
```

## Mistakes to avoid

- **Demographic determinism.** Treating "25–34, urban, $80k" as an explanation of behavior. It's a correlation at best, a stereotype at worst, and it silently smuggles bias into the product.
- **Segmenting by your product category.** "Milkshake buyers," "our power users" — defining the market as users of the thing you sell hides the larger market and the real competitors (including non-consumption).
- **Confusing behavioral data with the underlying need.** "They click export a lot" is a behavior; the _need_ ("I have to show this to my boss in a meeting") is the thing to design for. Behavior is a clue, not the answer.
- **Segments that aren't actionable.** Cuts so fine, so vague, or so overlapping that no two imply a different decision. A segmentation that doesn't change what you do is decoration.
- **One axis to rule them all.** Insisting everything be demographic (lazy) or everything be JTBD (purist). Different decisions need different cuts.
- **Treating the first-cut hypothesis as a finding.** Drawing demographic segments before research and then never testing them against behavior.

## Scoring guide: good vs. bad segmentation

A strong segmentation is **causal where it must be, actionable, distinct, evidence-built, and axis-matched to the decision**; a weak one is demographic-by-default, descriptive-only, overlapping, and assumed.

| Dimension | Bad (low score) | Good (high score) |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Axis vs. decision** | Demographic cut driving a _product_ decision | Axis matched to the decision — jobs/needs for build, demo/geo for reach |
| **Causality** | Correlation treated as cause ("Gen Z, so they want X") | Segments explain _why_ a choice is made; demo claims tested for causation |
| **Evidence** | Drawn from intuition or stock market segments | Built from analytics (behavioral) or interviews (needs/jobs) |
| **Actionability** | Segments imply the same response; no decision changes | Each segment implies a distinct product/positioning response |
| **Distinctness** | Overlapping, fuzzy, or product-category-shaped | Mutually distinct; not defined as "users of our product" |
| **Layering** | Single axis, no go-to-market bridge | Needs/jobs segments mapped onto demo/geo descriptors for reach + sizing |

### Worked contrast

```text
BAD  (demographic, product-shaped, non-causal)
  Segment A: "Millennials" — Segment B: "Gen X" — Segment C: "Boomers"
  Decision fed: which features to build.
  → Age doesn't cause the purchase; the segments imply no distinct product; bias creeps in.
   This is the milkshake mistake: segmenting the buyer, not the job.

GOOD (jobs-based for the build, demographic layered for reach)
  Job segments for a budgeting app, from 20 interviews:
    1. "Get out of debt without shame"        — needs guided payoff, no judgment, small wins
    2. "Keep a variable freelance income afloat" — needs cash-flow smoothing, tax set-aside
    3. "Coordinate money with a partner"        — needs shared view, permissions, fairness
  Each implies a DIFFERENT product response. Then, for go-to-market only, layer demographics:
   segment 2 skews 25–40 self-employed → tells you WHERE to advertise, not WHAT to build.
```

## Labeled uncertainty: a genuine fork in JTBD segmentation

How to define the "job" you segment on is **actively contested**, and the disagreement is load-bearing for segmentation. Tony Ulwick (Strategyn, Outcome-Driven Innovation) argues Clayton C.'s own milkshake example misapplies the theory: a long boring commute "is not a key task or goal. It is a context within which you execute a job," and the real job is "get breakfast on the go." Ulwick's method defines the job, decomposes it into steps, and then **"segment[s] the market based on differing ways in which customers… struggle to execute the job"** — i.e. by _unmet desired outcomes within one job_, not by different product uses (which he says Clayton C. wrongly labeled "segments"). So even among JTBD proponents there is no settled procedure for turning jobs into segments. Practical guidance: pick one lineage explicitly (Clayton C.'s progress-in-a-circumstance, or Ulwick's outcomes-within-a-job), be consistent, and don't assume two teams citing "JTBD segmentation" mean the same thing.
