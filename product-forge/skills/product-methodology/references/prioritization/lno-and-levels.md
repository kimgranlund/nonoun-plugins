# Shreyas D.'s Prioritization as a Working Method

Most prioritization advice scores _what to build_. Shreyas D.'s contribution is upstream of that: it scores **how much of yourself to spend** on each thing you've already decided to do, and it reframes the failures we blame on execution as failures of strategy. This reference treats his ideas as a working method — when to reach for each one, how to apply it, and the self-test that keeps it honest. The conceptual home is the methodology Canon; this file is the operating manual for the prioritization axis.

> The load-bearing insight, in his words: "all your tasks are not created equal." Effort is not the scarce resource — _well-placed_ effort is. Everything below follows from refusing to spread it evenly.

## The LNO framework: match effort to leverage, not to the task

Shreyas D. sorts every task you do into three buckets by the return your effort earns, then prescribes a _different quality bar_ for each. The buckets are not about importance or urgency — they are about the slope of the effort-to-impact curve.

| Bucket | Return on effort (Shreyas D.) | Quality bar he prescribes | His examples |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **L — Leverage** | "a 10x (or even 100x) return" | "put in a ton of hours and let your inner perfectionist shine" | product roadmap, strategy |
| **N — Neutral** | "just 1.1x return" | "do a solid job, nothing over the top" | prep for a partner call |
| **O — Overhead** | "necessary, but provide little return" | "actively try to do a bad job, because in reality, you won't" | email |

The "do a bad job" instruction for Overhead is deliberately provocative. Shreyas D.'s point is that a self-respecting professional _cannot_ actually do a bad job — aiming low on Overhead lands you at "good enough," whereas aiming high lands you at "gold-plated email" and steals the hours Leverage needed. The framework is a counterweight to perfectionism, which is why he frames it personally: "like many PMs, I'm a perfectionist. Or at least I used to be."

### How to apply it

1. **Bucket by curve, then per-task.** L/N/O is contextual — Shreyas D. is explicit that "what's an L vs an N or an O will vary." The same activity (say, writing a doc) can be Leverage in one situation and Overhead in another. Re-bucket per task; don't hard-code categories.
2. **Set the quality bar from the bucket, before you start.** Decide "this is an O, I'm aiming for good-enough" up front. The damage from perfectionism is done by drift, not by decision — you don't choose to gold-plate, you just fail to choose not to.
3. **Protect the Leverage slot first.** The framework's payoff is reallocation: reclaim hours from Overhead and N, and spend them where the curve is steep. Doing L work _well_ is the only place perfectionism pays.
4. **Watch your own resistance.** Shreyas D. flags emotional avoidance as a signal that you're dodging the high-leverage task, quoting Campbell: "the cave you fear to enter holds the treasure you seek." The task you keep not starting is often the L.

### The "is this Leverage, or Overhead dressed up?" test

The framework's failure mode is **mislabeling Overhead as Leverage** so you can lavish attention on comfortable, low-return work and feel productive. Run this test before pouring hours into anything:

- **Counterfactual return.** If I did this at _good-enough_ instead of _perfect_, what would actually be lost? If the honest answer is "little," it is Overhead — no matter how satisfying it feels. Real Leverage degrades badly at good-enough; that asymmetry is the tell.
- **Curve, not comfort.** Am I calling this high-leverage because the impact curve is steep, or because I _enjoy_ it / am _good_ at it / it's _legible_ to others? Enjoyment, skill, and visibility are not leverage. (The optics trap below is the same error wearing a different hat.)
- **The reallocation question.** What would I do with these hours if I capped this task at Neutral? If a clearly higher-return task exists and is going unstarted, the thing in front of you is Overhead by revealed preference.

A task only earns the Leverage label — and the perfectionism that comes with it — if it survives all three.

## The three levels of product work: impact, execution, optics

Shreyas D. calls this "a fundamental framework of product work." Any piece of work can be discussed at three levels, and most product conflict comes from people operating at different ones without naming it.

- **Impact** — the long-term effect the work has on the company and its customers. The level outcomes actually live at.
- **Execution** — the nuts and bolts of getting it done and shipped. The level most ICs default to and are measured on.
- **Optics** — how the work is perceived by others. Shreyas D.'s career lesson here is candid: he says one mistake he made was to "downplay the role of Optics." Optics is not inherently cynical; ignoring it is its own failure mode.

Each level matters, and "everyone has a default level they enjoy." The working move is **intentionality**: name the level you're operating at, and the level your stakeholder is operating at. Shreyas D.'s diagnosis of team friction: conflict arises "when team members are focused on different things and talking from different levels," and the fix is to figure out which level you ought to be communicating at with a given person. A PM defending _execution_ details to a CEO who is asking an _impact_ question will lose the room even when the work is good.

This level model is also the engine behind the LNO test above: Leverage work is overwhelmingly Impact-level; "Overhead dressed up as Leverage" is very often Optics-level work (fast, visible, flattering) misread as Impact.

## Most execution problems are strategy problems

Shreyas D.'s most-cited reframe: many failures we file under "execution" are symptoms of an unresolved problem one layer up. Teams thrash on _how_ to ship because no one settled _what_ or _why_. Patching the execution symptom — more standups, tighter tickets, a new tool — is, in his framing, a band-aid that leaves the wound.

The working diagnostic is to stop at "execution problem" and ask which underlying problem is actually showing through:

- a **strategy problem** — the team lacks clarity on the overall strategy, so local execution decisions have nothing to align to;
- a **cultural problem** — e.g. a culture that over-rewards individual OKRs, which structurally punishes the collaboration the work needs;
- an **interpersonal problem** — e.g. poor relationships between the people who must coordinate.

Naming the real layer is the whole move: it's what lets you spend a Leverage-grade fix on the actual cause instead of an Overhead-grade fix on the symptom. "We keep missing dates" is rarely solved by better date-tracking when the real issue is that two leads disagree on what's being built.

## Product sense as a skill

Shreyas D. treats product sense not as innate taste but as a developable skill — pattern recognition built deliberately from exposure, feedback, and reflection, the way any craft skill is. The practical consequence for prioritization: judging which tasks are genuinely Leverage, and which "execution" problems are really strategy problems, _is_ product sense in action — so it improves with the same deliberate practice, and a junior PM's mis-bucketing is a skill gap to close, not a fixed trait.

## ROI vs. opportunity-cost thinking

A complementary Shreyas D. reframe that sharpens the LNO buckets. He argues that ROI thinking biases teams toward "quick wins and low-hanging fruit because they are easier to execute" — high _ratio_, low _absolute_ impact. He advocates shifting to **opportunity cost**: the gap between the optimal outcome and the one you chose. Under opportunity-cost thinking, a comfortable quick win that crowds out a much larger bet is _expensive_, because its true cost is the better thing you didn't do. This is the same logic as the LNO reallocation question — the cost of over-investing in Overhead is the Leverage you forfeited — applied at the level of which initiatives make the roadmap at all.

## Pre-mortems (the paired practice)

In the same body of work Shreyas D. popularized the **pre-mortem** as a planning move: before committing, imagine the project has already failed and work backward to enumerate why. It's the risk-surfacing complement to LNO's effort-allocation — LNO tells you where to spend effort, the pre-mortem stress-tests whether the high-leverage bet is sound before you spend it. (Covered here only as a pointer; it belongs to the discovery/planning axis, not prioritization.)

## Complementary scoring frames: RICE and ICE

LNO governs _effort per task once chosen_; it is not a backlog-scoring model. For ranking _which_ items to pursue, two lightweight frames compose with it:

- **ICE** — score each idea on **Impact x Confidence x Ease** and rank by the product. (Popularized by Sean Ellis in growth-experiment prioritization.)
- **RICE** — **(Reach x Impact x Confidence) / Effort**, a single comparable score per initiative. (Introduced by Intercom for prioritizing product roadmaps.)

How they fit together: use RICE/ICE to decide _what_ enters the roadmap (cross-item ranking), the three-levels model to make sure you're ranking on **Impact** and not Optics, then LNO to decide _how much of yourself each chosen task gets_. RICE/ICE answer "is this worth doing?"; LNO answers "now that we're doing it, is it Leverage or Overhead?" Shreyas D.'s own caution applies on top of all three: a high score on an easy win can still be a poor choice once you price in opportunity cost.

> Attribution note: RICE (Intercom) and ICE (Sean Ellis) are widely-used industry frames, included here as complementary context per the brief — they are _not_ Shreyas D.'s frameworks and he is not their source.
