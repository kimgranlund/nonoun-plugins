# Jobs-to-be-Done (Clayton C.-canonical)

Jobs-to-be-Done (JTBD) is a lens for explaining _why_ customers choose what they choose. Clayton C.'s framing — set out in _Competing Against Luck_ (2016) and the companion HBR article "Know Your Customers' Jobs to Be Done" — holds that people don't buy products and services; they **hire** them to make progress in a particular circumstance. The unit of analysis is the job, not the product, the customer, or the market. This file is deliberately kept Clayton C.-canonical; see the note on variants at the end.

## The core claim: customers hire products to make progress

Clayton C.'s foundational move is to relocate causality. A purchase is not caused by a product's attributes, nor by who the customer is — it is caused by a _job_ the customer is trying to get done. Clayton C.'s definition of a job: **the progress that a person is trying to make in a particular circumstance.** Progress is directional (a person moving from where they are toward where they want to be), and it is always embedded in a circumstance that supplies the constraints and the standard for "better."

The hiring metaphor is load-bearing, not decorative. When a job arises, a customer reaches into their life, surveys whatever is available, and **hires** something to do the job. When it does the job poorly, they **fire** it and hire something else. So the relevant question is never "what features should we add?" but "what job did the customer hire this for, and what would make us the best possible hire?"

Clayton C. credits Theodore Levitt with the seed idea, usually quoted as: "People don't want to buy a quarter-inch drill. They want a quarter-inch hole." The drill (the product) is incidental; the hole (the progress) is the point — and even the hole is only a proxy for some further progress (a shelf hung, a room made livable).

## A job is progress, not attributes or demographics

The discipline of the theory is mostly in what it _refuses_ to treat as causal:

- **Not product attributes.** Adding features to a product tells you nothing about whether it does a job better. Features only matter insofar as they advance the progress the customer is after.
- **Not customer demographics.** Clayton C. is blunt that demographic and psychographic correlations — age, income, segment — describe _who_ buys without explaining _why_. They are correlation masquerading as cause. A 35-year-old suburban parent and a 22-year-old commuter can hire the same product for the same job; the same person hires different products for the same job in different circumstances.
- **Not the category as conventionally drawn.** The set of things competing for a job rarely matches the official "category." The true competitive set is everything the customer might hire for that job, including non-consumption (doing nothing) and crude workarounds.

A well-specified job has three dimensions that travel together — **functional** (the practical task to accomplish), **social** (how it positions the person in the eyes of others), and **emotional** (how the person wants to feel). Strong products satisfy all three; products that nail only the functional dimension are easy to fire.

## The milkshake study

The milkshake case is the canonical illustration, drawn from work Clayton C.'s collaborators (including Bob Moesta of The Re-Wired Group) conducted for a fast-food chain trying to sell more milkshakes. Conventional research — improving the product along attributes that the existing milkshake-buying demographic said they wanted (thicker, cheaper, chunkier) — moved nothing.

Reframing around the job produced the insight. Researchers observed _when_ milkshakes were bought and asked what customers were trying to get done. They found two distinct jobs in two distinct circumstances:

- **The morning commute.** A large share of milkshakes sold early in the morning, bought by solo commuters who took them to go. The job: roughly, _help me stay awake, occupied, and not-yet-hungry through a long, boring commute._ In this circumstance the milkshake's real competitors were not other milkshakes but **bananas, doughnuts, bagels, Snickers bars, and coffee** — each of which lost on some dimension (a banana is gone too fast; a doughnut leaves crumbs and greasy fingers on the wheel; coffee doesn't fill you up). A thick milkshake sucked slowly through a straw won precisely _because_ it was viscous and one-handed and lasted the whole drive. The "flaw" was the feature.
- **The afternoon treat.** A second cluster of milkshakes sold in the afternoon to parents, as a way to say yes to a child after a day of saying no. Here, as the case puts it, "the milkshake isn't competing against a banana or a Snickers bar or a doughnut… It's competing against stopping at the toy store." Same product, entirely different job, entirely different competitive set — and the right product response (smaller, faster) was the opposite of the morning answer.

The lesson Clayton C. draws: **"People hired milkshakes for two very different jobs during the day, in two very different circumstances. Each job has a very different set of competitors."** Optimizing the _product_ in the abstract is incoherent because there is no single job to optimize for. You can only improve a product _against a job in a circumstance._

## How to uncover the job

JTBD is closer to investigative reporting than to surveying. Clayton C.'s practice emphasizes finding the job's _whole context_, especially the moment of struggle that triggered a switch. Useful sources of evidence:

- **Watch the moment of hire and the moment of struggle.** Interview people about a specific recent purchase: what was happening just before, what pushed them to act _that day_, what they were trying to accomplish, what they had tried before and abandoned. The aim is a documentary-grade timeline of the switch, not a satisfaction score.
- **Study non-consumption and workarounds.** People hacking together a solution out of duct tape, spreadsheets, or doing nothing reveal a job that is being served badly. Non-consumption is often the largest competitor.
- **Look at the four forces of progress** (from the JTBD switching model Clayton C. presents): the **push** of the present situation and the **pull** of the new solution drive a switch; the **anxiety** about the new and the **habit/inertia** of the present hold it back. A purchase happens when push + pull overcome anxiety + habit. Designing for a job means amplifying push/pull _and_ defusing anxiety/habit.
- **Find the competitive set the customer actually weighs** — across categories — by asking what else they considered and what they'd do if your product vanished.
- **Separate the dimensions.** Explicitly probe functional, social, and emotional progress; the social and emotional dimensions are where weakly-differentiated products lose.

Clayton C.'s promise is that this work makes innovation less of a gamble: "If you understand the job, how to improve the product becomes just obvious." The title's argument is that firms otherwise innovate by luck.

## The "what job, what circumstance" test

A one-line test to check whether a stated job is actually Clayton C.-canonical, or has quietly slid back into attributes/demographics:

```text
Can you finish this sentence with progress (a verb), not a feature or a persona?

  "When I am [CIRCUMSTANCE], I want to [MAKE THIS PROGRESS],
   so I can [LARGER PROGRESS / outcome]."

Then sanity-check it:
  - Circumstance: is there a concrete triggering situation (when/where/what just happened)?
  - Progress: is it a verb — a change of state the person wants — not an attribute of your product?
  - Competitive set: list everything that could be HIRED for this job,
    including "do nothing." If your product is the only entry, the job is too narrow.
  - Demographic check: would the job survive if you deleted all age/income/segment labels?
    If it depends on a persona to make sense, it's a demographic, not a job.
```

Fails to watch for: a "job" that names your product ("the job is to use our dashboard") — that's an attribute; a "job" that is really a customer type ("busy millennials") — that's a demographic; a "job" with no circumstance ("be productive") — too abstract to design against or to identify competitors for.

## Note on variants (kept out of this file by design)

"Jobs-to-be-Done" is not a single school. Clayton C.'s progress-in-a-circumstance framing (this file) is one of three commonly conflated lineages: **Tony Ulwick's Outcome-Driven Innovation (ODI)** treats a job as a fixed process decomposed into measurable "desired outcomes" and is far more quantitative; **Bob Moesta and Chris Spiek's "Jobs-to-be-Done" interview practice** (the switch interview, the four forces, the timeline of demand) grew out of the same milkshake work but is its own method. These overlap and sometimes contradict. This file is intentionally Clayton C.-canonical — when a teammate cites "JTBD," confirm which lineage they mean before reconciling definitions. (Labeled here as a known fork, not a settled synthesis.)
