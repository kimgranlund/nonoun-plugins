# JTBD variants: Clayton C. vs. Ulwick vs. Moesta

"Jobs-to-be-Done" is not one method. It is a name shared by at least three lineages that agree on a slogan — _people don't buy products, they hire them to get a job done_ — and then diverge, sometimes contradicting each other, on what a "job" is, how you find it, and what you do with it. Treating them as interchangeable is the single most common JTBD mistake. This file maps the genuine differences, says when to reach for each, and — importantly — labels the disagreements that are _not_ settled rather than papering over them. Read `christensen-jtbd.md` and `ulwick-odi.md` first for each camp's canon; this is the comparison.

## The three lineages at a glance

|  | **Clayton C. (Jobs Theory)** | **Ulwick (ODI)** | **Moesta (Demand-Side)** |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Canonical text | _Competing Against Luck_ (2016) | _Jobs to be Done: Theory to Practice_ (2016) | _Demand-Side Sales 101_ (2020) |
| What a "job" is | **Progress** a person seeks in a circumstance | A **stable process / task** with measurable outcomes | The **progress** that triggers a switch (purchase) |
| Stance | A **theory / lens** (the _why_) | A **method** (the _how_, quantified) | A **method** (the _how_, qualitative) |
| Unit of analysis | The job (progress + functional/social/emotional) | ~50–150 **desired outcomes** per job | The **switch** — fire the old, hire the new |
| Core research move | Documentary interview about the struggle | Job map -> outcome survey (importance x satisfaction) | The **Switch interview** (timeline of one purchase) |
| Method texture | **Qualitative**, conceptual | **Quantitative**, statistical | **Qualitative**, behavioral |
| Signature artifact | The four forces; functional/social/emotional | Job map (8 steps); opportunity landscape | Timeline of demand; four forces; six stages |
| Best for | Strategy, framing, "why do we exist" | Resource allocation, finding under-served needs | Sales, onboarding, reducing switching friction |
| Output you act on | A reframed job + competitive set | A ranked list of under-/over-served outcomes | Intervention points along the buying timeline |

## The genuine differences (not stylistic)

**1. Is a job _progress_ or a _process_? (the deepest fork.)** Clayton C. and Moesta define a job as **progress** — a change a person is trying to create in their life, irreducibly bound up with emotional and social dimensions, not just the functional task. Ulwick defines a job as a **stable functional process** that can be mapped into steps and decomposed into measurable outcomes; emotion enters only as additional (rateable) outcome statements layered on the functional core. This is not two phrasings of one idea. Alan Klement, who sits in the progress camp, labels the two readings **"Jobs-as-Progress"** versus **"Jobs-as-Activities"** and argues they are genuinely different interpretations that lead to different research and different products. A team that says "let's do JTBD" without resolving this fork will produce incoherent work because the two readings ask different questions on day one.

**2. Qualitative depth vs. quantitative rigor.** Ulwick's method is explicitly statistical: you survey a large sample, rate every outcome for importance and satisfaction, and cluster the results to find under-served outcomes and outcome-based segments. Clayton C.'s and Moesta's methods are explicitly qualitative: a small number of deep interviews reconstructing the _causal story_ of a real decision. These optimize different risks — Ulwick guards against acting on a vivid anecdote that doesn't generalize; Clayton C./Moesta guard against measuring the wrong thing precisely. Neither is "more rigorous" in the abstract; they are rigorous about different failures.

**3. Demographics: all three reject them, for different reasons.** Every lineage refuses to let age/income/segment _cause_ a purchase. But the replacement differs: Clayton C. replaces demographics with **circumstance + progress**; Ulwick replaces them with **outcome-based segments** (clusters of customers with different unmet-outcome patterns); Moesta replaces them with **where the buyer sits on the switching timeline**. Same enemy, three different substitutes — and the substitutes are not equivalent.

**4. What you ship at the end.** Clayton C. hands you a **reframed job and an honest competitive set** (the milkshake's rivals are bananas and boredom) — strategic input, not a build list. Ulwick hands you a **ranked, quantified opportunity list** — under-served outcomes to invest in, over-served outcomes to cut — which is close to a roadmap. Moesta hands you a **map of the buying journey with intervention points** — closer to a sales-and-onboarding playbook than a product spec. If a team adopts "JTBD" expecting a roadmap and runs Clayton C.-style interviews, they will feel the method "didn't give an answer" — because that lineage was never meant to.

## The common confusions (label them, don't blend them)

- **Conflating "progress" with "outcomes."** Clayton C.'s _progress_ includes emotional and social change and is holistic; Ulwick's _outcomes_ are discrete, solution-free success metrics. Borrowing Ulwick's tidy outcome syntax to express Clayton C.'s progress quietly strips out the emotional/social dimension that was Clayton C.'s whole point.
- **Assuming one interview method.** Clayton C. and Moesta both interview qualitatively, but for different ends — Clayton C. to understand the _job and its competitive set_, Moesta to reconstruct the _causal timeline of a switch_. Ulwick interviews only to _discover_ outcomes, then validates them with a survey. "We did JTBD interviews" tells you almost nothing until you say _which_ kind.
- **Treating the four forces as universal JTBD furniture.** The push/pull/anxiety/habit "four forces of progress" come from the Clayton C./Moesta switching tradition (developed largely by Moesta and Spiek out of the milkshake work). They are not part of Ulwick's ODI machinery. Citing the four forces and the opportunity algorithm in one breath as "the JTBD framework" fuses two methods that don't share a backbone.
- **Believing the job definitions are the same.** They are not: Ulwick — "cut the wood in a straight line" (task with measurable criteria); Clayton C. — "make progress toward the life I want, in this circumstance" (contextual, psychological); Moesta — "I'm stuck deciding and need to move" (demand-activated). Three definitions wearing one name.
- **Crediting the milkshake study to a single school.** The milkshake work is shared origin tissue — Clayton C. popularized it, and Moesta (then at the Re-Wired Group) did much of the underlying fieldwork. It is _not_ evidence for Ulwick's quantitative method, which has a separate lineage going back to 1991.

## When to use which

```text
You want to ...                                        Reach for ...
-----------------------------------------------------  --------------------------
Reframe a market / find the real competitive set       Clayton C. (Jobs Theory)
Decide "why do we exist" and how to position           Clayton C.  (+ April D.)
Rank a backlog of needs by quantified opportunity      Ulwick (ODI)
Find under-served vs over-served needs at scale        Ulwick (ODI)
Build a needs-based segmentation                       Ulwick (ODI)
Understand why buyers switch (or don't)                Moesta (Demand-Side)
Fix conversion / onboarding / churn at decision points Moesta (Demand-Side)
Run a single deep "what happened the day you bought"   Moesta (Switch interview)
```

A common, defensible combination in practice: **Clayton C./Moesta qualitative interviews to understand demand and the switch, then Ulwick-style outcome mapping to quantify which gaps are worth closing.** This pairing is pragmatic, but note it is a _practitioner blend_, not a reconciliation the founders endorse — which is the next section.

## What is genuinely unresolved (labeled honestly)

These are open disagreements, not settled questions. Do not present them as resolved:

- **Whether the lineages are even compatible.** The progress camp (Klement, and broadly the Clayton C./Moesta tradition) argues that Jobs-as-Activities and Jobs-as-Progress are _different theories_, not two altitudes of one — i.e., they cannot simply be stacked. The pragmatic middle ground ("interview qualitatively, then quantify with ODI") is widely practiced but is a **working compromise asserted by practitioners**, not an agreement among the originators. _Single-source / contested:_ treat any claim that the three "are really the same thing at different zoom levels" as a contested opinion, not a fact.
- **Provenance and credit.** Who influenced Clayton C., and how much, is itself disputed. Ulwick's outcome-driven work predates _Competing Against Luck_ by years; Moesta did fieldwork behind the canonical examples. There are competing narratives about lineage and ownership (Ulwick's approach is patented; others contest the framing). This reference does not adjudicate the credit dispute — it only flags that one exists and that sources are partisan.
- **Whether emotion is "an outcome" or "the point."** Ulwick can express emotional and social needs as additional outcome statements; Clayton C. treats the emotional/social dimensions as constitutive of progress, not as add-on metrics. Whether those are the same claim in different notation, or a real philosophical disagreement about what a job _is_, is unresolved — and it is exactly the disagreement teams paper over when they "merge" the frameworks.

Practical guidance under uncertainty: **name the lineage explicitly before you start.** When a teammate says "JTBD," the first question is "which one — Clayton C., Ulwick, or Moesta?" Most cross-team JTBD friction is two people running different methods under one word.
