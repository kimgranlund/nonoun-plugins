# Continuous Discovery as a Working Method

Continuous discovery is not a research phase, a quarterly ritual, or something a researcher does to a backlog. It is a **habit the building team holds every week**. This reference defines the habit precisely enough that you can tell — this week — whether you are actually doing it, and converts the idea into a cadence, a team shape, and an interview technique you can run on Monday.

> Teresa T.'s working definition: continuous discovery is **"at a minimum, weekly touchpoints with customers, by the team building the product, where they conduct small research activities, in pursuit of a desired product outcome."** Every clause is load-bearing. Drop any one and you are doing something else with a discovery sticker on it.

---

## The four clauses, and what each one rejects

The definition is a four-part test. Read it as a filter: each clause names a common substitute and rules it out.

| Clause | What it requires | What it rejects |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **At a minimum, weekly** | A standing, recurring touchpoint — not "when we have a question." Cadence beats batch size. | The quarterly research sprint; the big study before a big bet; discovery as a launch gate. |
| **Touchpoints with customers** | Contact with the actual people who use or buy the product. | Talking _about_ customers using analytics, sales anecdotes, or a persona deck as a stand-in for a live human. |
| **By the team building the product** | The trio that decides and builds does the talking — not a research team that hands over a report. | Outsourced discovery; insight that arrives as a PDF the makers did not witness. |
| **Small research activities** | Lightweight, repeatable moves — one or two interviews, one assumption test — sized to fit a normal week. | The monolithic study; the 40-question survey; research so heavy it can only run occasionally. |
| **In pursuit of a desired product outcome** | A specific behavioral target that scopes what you go looking for. | Open-ended "learning" with no outcome to steer it; talking to users to feel diligent. |

The unifying point: **discovery is defined by frequency and ownership, not by volume.** A team doing one customer conversation every week, every week, is doing continuous discovery. A team doing thirty interviews once a year is not — it is doing a study.

## The product trio

The habit is held by a small cross-functional group Teresa T. calls the **product trio**: a **product manager, a designer, and a software engineer** — the people who, together, decide what to build and then build it. (Teresa T. notes the trio commonly draws on others — user researchers, data analysts, domain experts — but the three roles are the core that must share the discovery work.)

Why three roles, together, and not a hand-off:

- **Shared context, not relayed context.** When the trio interviews together, they build a common evidence base and stop arguing from competing summaries. Insight degrades every time it is retold; co-witnessing removes the relay.
- **Three lenses on the same story.** The PM hears the opportunity, the designer hears the experience, the engineer hears feasibility and edge cases — in one pass, on the same customer.
- **Decision authority sits with the listeners.** The people who will choose the bet are the people who heard the need. Discovery and the decision are not separated by a document.

> **Test for the trio.** After a customer conversation, can all three members independently recount the same specific story the customer told? If only one was in the room — or if "discovery" is a thing one role does and reports — you have a research function, not a product trio.

## The discovery / delivery cadence

Teresa T. draws a clean line between the two kinds of work a product team does, and the central claim of the method is that they run **in parallel**, continuously — not as sequential phases.

- **Discovery** — _"the work that we do to decide what to build."_
- **Delivery** — _"the work that we do to build, ship, and maintain a production-level product."_

```text
        ┌──────────── DISCOVERY (continuous) ────────────┐
week →  interview · assumption test · interview · test · interview · …
        └────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
        ┌──────────── DELIVERY (continuous) ─────────────┐
week →  build · ship · build · ship · build · ship · …
        └────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
                 ↑ both run every week, informing each other
```

The anti-pattern this replaces is **"discovery, then delivery"** — a gate where a team researches for a quarter, declares the answer known, then builds for two quarters without talking to anyone. In that model discovery is a phase you exit. In continuous discovery it is a rhythm you never leave: this week's delivery raises this week's questions, and this week's interviews reshape what gets built next. The two streams are concurrent and mutually correcting.

This does not mean infinite research before any build. It means **small, steady learning alongside shipping**, so that bets are corrected by contact with reality every week instead of validated once and then defended.

## Small research activities: the two core moves

"Small research activities" is not vague. Teresa T. names two activities that make up the bulk of the weekly habit:

1. **Interviewing to discover opportunities** — surfacing unmet customer needs, pain points, and desires from real experience. (These opportunities become the middle layer of the opportunity solution tree; see the companion reference.)
2. **Assumption testing to evaluate solutions** — small experiments that test the riskiest assumptions behind a candidate solution, _before_ committing to build it.

The first feeds the **opportunity space** (what to address); the second feeds the **solution space** (whether a given idea will work). Both are sized to run inside a normal week — one or two conversations, one quick test — which is precisely what makes the weekly cadence sustainable.

## Interviewing for stories, not opinions

The single most consequential interviewing move in the method: **collect specific stories about past behavior, not opinions, generalizations, or predictions.** This is the difference between an interview that yields a real opportunity and one that yields a comforting fiction.

The reasoning, in Teresa T.'s framing:

- When people are asked to **generalize** ("what do you usually do," "what do you like"), they answer fast, from **System 1** — automatic, low-effort thinking that "saves energy and effort, but is prone to errors (e.g. cognitive biases)."
- Those fast answers are distorted by **aspirational bias** (people describe their idealized selves), **recency bias**, and the **availability heuristic** — they report the easy-to-recall, not the true. Ask what someone likes to watch and you hear "documentaries" while last night's reality TV goes unmentioned.
- A **specific past story** sidesteps this. It is concrete, datable, and hard to idealize on the spot.

The contrast in practice:

| Question shape | What it produces | Verdict |
| --- | --- | --- |
| "What do you like/dislike about X?" · "What do you usually do?" | A summary, a generalization, an opinion — System 1, idealized. | **Reject.** Not evidence of behavior. |
| "Tell me about the **last time** you did X." | A specific story, from beginning to end, grounded in real past behavior. | **Use.** This is the unit of discovery. |

A worked example from Teresa T.: ask the generalizing question and you get _"I like to download shows on my iPhone."_ Open with **"Tell me about the last time you watched Netflix"** and you get the real, usable thing — _"I downloaded episodes to my phone ahead of time so that I could watch Breaking Bad on my flight."_ The second answer carries context, motivation, and constraint; the first carries none.

Two operating rules follow:

- **Open with a specific instance**, then follow the story from beginning to end. The goal is _"a specific story from beginning to end"_ grounded in real past behavior — not hypotheticals about the future.
- **Let the participant do most of the talking.** The interviewer's craft is knowing what to ask, and when, so the person keeps narrating their actual experience rather than theorizing about it.

## The "are you actually doing it?" test

Continuous discovery is easy to claim and easy to fake. Run this checklist against the **last seven days**, not against intentions or the org chart:

- [ ] **Weekly contact, actually.** Did the team talk to at least one real customer in the past week? (If the honest answer is "not this week, we were busy shipping," the habit has lapsed — that is the failure mode the method is built to catch.)
- [ ] **The makers were in the room.** Was it the PM/designer/engineer who spoke with the customer — not a report they read?
- [ ] **Stories, not opinions.** Did the conversation produce a specific story about something the customer actually did, or a list of preferences and predictions?
- [ ] **Pointed at an outcome.** Was the conversation in service of a defined product outcome, or open-ended "learning" with nothing to steer it?
- [ ] **Sustainable, not heroic.** Was it small enough to repeat next week — or a one-off heavy push you cannot maintain?

If any box is unchecked, you are not yet doing continuous discovery — you are doing periodic research, or talking about customers, or running a study. The fix is rarely "do more research." It is almost always **automate the recruiting** so a customer conversation lands on the calendar every week without a fresh act of will, and shrink each activity until it fits a normal week.

> The bright-line rule: **cadence is the practice.** A team that talks to one customer every week, indefinitely, is doing continuous discovery. A team that runs a brilliant thirty-interview study once a year is not. Frequency and ownership are the method; volume is not.
