# The Opportunity Solution Tree as a Working Artifact

The opportunity solution tree (OST) is Teresa T.'s visual model for product discovery вҖ” _"a simple way of visually representing the paths you might take to reach a desired outcome."_ It is not a slide. It is a **living artifact the product trio maintains and argues over**, the structural enforcement of the rule that you do not jump to a solution before you understand the need. This reference defines its four layers, how to build one in order, and how to tell a tree that is doing its job from one that is theater.

> What the tree is _for_: it helps the trio _"develop and share a mental representation of their combined knowledge,"_ _"makes implicit assumptions explicit,"_ and forces _"strong logical connections between the solutions they're exploring, the opportunities they've identified, and the impact both have on their desired outcome."_ Every solution must trace, by an explicit edge, back up to a customer need and then to the outcome вҖ” or it does not belong on the tree.

---

1. **Outcome (the root).** One desired result at the top. It _"sets the scope for discovery"_ вҖ” it decides which opportunities are even relevant and keeps the team focused on the right class of solutions. A tree has one outcome at a time.
2. **Opportunities (the middle).** Customer needs, pain points, and desires that, _if addressed_, would move the outcome. These are discovered through interviews, not invented in a meeting.
3. **Solutions.** Products, features, or changes that address a _specific_ opportunity. Every solution connects to exactly one opportunity; a solution that maps to no opportunity is off-strategy by construction.
4. **Assumption tests.** Small experiments that probe the **riskiest assumptions behind a solution** вҖ” not the whole solution. They live on their own row so the team is pushed to design _sets_ of tests per solution rather than one big bet.

## Get the outcome right first: three altitudes

Most bad trees are bad because they are rooted in the wrong kind of metric. Teresa T. distinguishes three altitudes, and only the middle one is the correct root for a discovery tree.

| Altitude | Example | Use as the tree root? |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Business outcome** | Revenue, market share, churn rate | **Too high.** A lagging financial result; the team cannot act on it directly. Use it to _justify_ the product outcome above the tree. |
| **Product outcome** | A change in **customer behavior or sentiment** the team can influence (e.g. "increase the number of users who complete onboarding") | **Yes вҖ” the right scope for discovery.** Actionable, owned by the trio, scopes the opportunity space. |
| **Traction metric** | Adoption of a single feature | **Too low.** Pre-commits to one solution and collapses the opportunity space before you have explored it. |

> Rule: **root the tree in a product outcome.** Too high and the team cannot act; too low and you have already chosen the answer. The outcome is the lever the whole tree exists to move.

## Building a tree, in order

Teresa T. lays out a deliberate sequence. The order is the point вҖ” doing these steps out of order is how teams jump to solutions.

1. **Check prerequisites.** Have a theory of your target customer, a defined product outcome, and a handful of customer interviews (вүҲ3вҖ“4) already done. Do not start the tree from zero.
2. **Place the outcome at the top.** One product outcome. This sets scope for everything below.
3. **Map the opportunity space from interview stories.** Extract needs, pains, and desires from the specific stories customers told (see the story-based interviewing rule in the continuous-discovery reference). Structure them as a set вҖ” sized, distinct, and grouped where they share a parent.
4. **Select one target opportunity.** Assess which opportunity is most worth pursuing now (importance to the customer, leverage on the outcome). Pick **one**.
5. **Brainstorm solutions for that opportunity only.** Generate options against the single target opportunity вҖ” not against the whole tree, not against "the product." Constraining the brainstorm to one need is what keeps it grounded.
6. **Choose about three solutions to explore.** Carry more than one candidate forward. Comparing several is what prevents falling in love with the first idea.
7. **Decompose each solution into its underlying assumptions.** Make implicit assumptions explicit вҖ” desirability, viability, feasibility, usability.
8. **Test the riskiest assumptions across the candidates.** Run small assumption tests against the assumptions most likely to be wrong and most fatal if they are вҖ” not against the whole solution.
9. **Evaluate and decide.** Based on the evidence, build, iterate, or pivot вҖ” then return to the tree and keep going. The tree is never "finished."

## How the tree prevents jumping to solutions

The tree's whole value is that it makes solution-first thinking structurally awkward. Four mechanisms do the work:

- **You must map before you choose.** Opportunities are laid out as a space _before_ a target is selected, so the team sees alternatives instead of fixating on the first need that came to mind.
- **Brainstorming is fenced to one opportunity.** Ideas are generated against a single, specific customer need вҖ” never against "the product" in the abstract вҖ” which keeps every solution tethered to a real problem.
- **You carry several solutions, not one.** Exploring вүҲ3 candidates in parallel defeats first-idea attachment; the team compares rather than commits.
- **You test assumptions before you build.** Decomposing solutions into assumptions and probing the riskiest ones means commitment is earned by evidence, not by enthusiasm.

The deeper guarantee is the **connected edges**: every solution traces up to an opportunity and every opportunity up to the outcome. A favorite feature with no opportunity above it has nowhere to attach вҖ” the artifact itself reveals it as off-strategy, before anyone spends a sprint on it.

## Good tree vs. bad tree

A quick diagnostic. If the right-hand column describes your tree, the structure is present but the discipline is not.

| Dimension | Good tree | Bad tree |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Source of opportunities** | Drawn from specific customer interview stories | Invented in an internal brainstorm; assumed |
| **Are "opportunities" real needs?** | Framed as customer needs ("customers need a faster workflow") | Solutions in disguise ("build feature X") |
| **Solutions per opportunity** | Multiple candidates explored and compared | A single, pre-chosen solution |
| **Outcome altitude** | A product outcome вҖ” actionable, owned by the trio | A business metric (too high) or feature adoption (too low) |
| **State over time** | Stabilizes and gets refined as interviews accumulate | Stays messy and unstructured well past the first several interviews |
| **Edges** | Every node connects up вҖ” solution вҶ’ opportunity вҶ’ outcome | Orphan solutions with nothing above them |

> **The disguise test for opportunities.** If there is only _one_ way to address something, it is a **solution wearing an opportunity costume** вҖ” file it in the solution layer. A genuine opportunity is a need broad enough to admit several different solutions. "Reduce time-to-first-value" is an opportunity; "add a setup wizard" is one solution to it.

## Operating notes

- **One tree, one outcome, the whole trio.** The tree is built and maintained by the product trio together вҖ” it is their shared mental model, not the PM's private diagram. If only one person touches it, it stops doing its alignment job.
- **It is a map, not a Gantt chart.** The tree represents _paths you might take_, not a committed sequence. Expect it to be re-drawn as interviews change what you know.
- **Messiness early is normal; messiness late is a smell.** The opportunity space is genuinely chaotic for the first few interviews. If it is still unstructured after six to eight, the team is collecting rather than synthesizing.
- **Effort estimates are excluded at selection time.** Choose the target opportunity by customer importance and leverage on the outcome first; weigh implementation effort when choosing _among solutions_, not when choosing which need to pursue.
