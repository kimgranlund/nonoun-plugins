# Positioning as a working method (April D.)

April D.'s _Obviously Awesome_ (2019) treats positioning not as a tagline or a brand mood, but as **the context you deliberately place a product in so that its value becomes obvious to the customers who should care most.** Her central, contrarian claim: positioning is **chosen, not assumed.** Most products ship with default positioning inherited from how the founders built it or how they first imagined it — and that default is almost always weaker than a positioning you would arrive at deliberately. April D.'s method is a procedure for replacing the inherited default with a chosen position.

## Positioning is context, and customers reason by analogy

April D.'s working definition: positioning **"defines how your product is the best in the world at delivering some kind of value that a well-defined set of customers cares a lot about."** Note the four moving parts inside it — _best in the world / at some value / a well-defined set of customers / who care a lot_ — which the five components below operationalize.

The mechanism behind why positioning matters: customers understand a new, unfamiliar product by **comparing it to things they already understand.** They look at your product, decide what "kind of thing" it is, and immediately import a set of expectations — about who it's for, what it competes with, what it should cost, what features it ought to have. If you don't choose the frame of reference, the customer chooses one for you, often the wrong one, and then your product looks confusing, underfeatured, or overpriced _relative to the wrong comparison._ Positioning is the act of choosing that frame so the comparison flatters what you're actually best at.

This is why April D. insists positioning is deliberate. The trap she names repeatedly is **default positioning**: "robot guys who built robots" position themselves as a robot, because that's the lens they built through — even when the value customers actually buy, and the competition they actually beat, would be obvious only under a different category. Founding vision and build-history are the _least_ reliable guides to the right frame.

## The five components (in order — the order is the method)

April D.'s framework is five interlocking components, and the **sequence is not optional**: each one is defined in terms of the previous, so working them out of order produces incoherent positioning. Work them in this order:

1. **Competitive alternatives** — _what your target customer would use or do if your product did not exist._ This is the starting point and the most commonly skipped step. Alternatives are frequently **not** other vendors: the real alternative is often **doing nothing** (living with the problem / non-consumption), a **spreadsheet**, a **homegrown internal script**, or **hiring an intern / an analyst** to do the work by hand. You can only know what's special about your product relative to what the customer would otherwise reach for. Get the alternatives wrong and every downstream component is wrong.

2. **Unique attributes** — _the features and capabilities you have that the alternatives do not._ These can be product features, but also delivery model, business model, data, integrations, or domain expertise. The test is strict: an attribute only counts if the alternatives in step 1 lack it. (This is why step 1 must come first.)

3. **Value (and proof)** — _the benefit your unique attributes deliver to the customer — the "so what?"_ Every unique attribute is mapped to a concrete value the customer cares about (saved time, won deals, avoided risk, satisfied compliance). April D. insists value be **fact-based and provable**, not adjectival — backed by data, demonstrations, or third-party validation rather than self-description. Value is the bridge from "what we have that others don't" to "why anyone should care."

4. **Best-fit customers** — _the characteristics of the buyers who care a lot about that value._ These are defined **by the value**, not by demographics or firmographics for their own sake: the best-fit customers are the segment at the intersection of all your unique value — the buyers who purchase fast, don't haggle on price, and become champions. Identify them by the pain points that make your specific value matter most to them.

5. **Market category** — _the market you frame your product as part of, so the value is obvious to the best-fit customers._ Category is the strongest single lever on perception, because naming a category triggers a whole bundle of customer assumptions (who it's for, what it costs, what features are table-stakes, what it competes with). Choose the category that makes your unique value look obvious and your strengths look like the point — and be aware that the wrong category silently imports the wrong expectations and the wrong comparisons.

The components form a chain: **alternatives → attributes (vs. those alternatives) → value (of those attributes) → customers (who care about that value) → category (that makes the value obvious to them).** Pull on the wrong alternative or the wrong category and the whole chain mis-resolves.

## "Do nothing" and "the intern" — taking alternatives seriously

The most actionable and most-skipped idea in the method is that **your competition is rarely the competitor you fixate on.** April D. pushes teams to enumerate what the customer _actually_ does today:

- **Do nothing / status quo** — the customer keeps living with the problem. This is the dominant alternative for genuinely new products and is the one teams forget, because "do nothing" doesn't show up in a competitive grid.
- **A spreadsheet** — the generic stand-in for "they cobbled it together themselves with tools they already have."
- **Hiring a person — an intern, an analyst, an agency** — the customer throws labor at the problem instead of software.

Naming these honestly does two things: it reveals the unique attributes that _actually_ matter (the ones a spreadsheet or an intern can't match), and it stops you from positioning against a sophisticated competitor on dimensions the customer isn't even weighing yet. If the only alternative is "do nothing," your category and value story must first establish that the problem is worth solving at all — a different job than out-positioning a rival.

## The "name the alternative you beat + the category you win" test

A fast check that positioning is actually chosen and coherent, not inherited:

```text
Fill in both blanks with specifics, then check the chain:

  "For [BEST-FIT CUSTOMER], who [PAIN that makes them care],
   we are a [MARKET CATEGORY]
   that, unlike [the COMPETITIVE ALTERNATIVE they'd otherwise use],
   delivers [PROVABLE VALUE] because [UNIQUE ATTRIBUTE the alternative lacks]."

Checks:
  - Alternative: is it concrete and honest — including "do nothing", a spreadsheet,
    or hiring someone — not just the vendor you like to compare against?
  - Category: would your best-fit customer, hearing this category, import expectations
    that FLATTER your strengths? If the category makes you look underfeatured or
    overpriced, it's the wrong frame — choose another.
  - Chain integrity: does each clause depend on the previous one?
    (attribute beats the named alternative → value comes from that attribute →
     customer is defined by caring about that value → category makes it obvious)
  - Default-check: is this position chosen, or is it just "what we built"?
    If it merely restates your build-history or founding vision, you have default
    positioning, not a deliberate one.
```

Failure modes to watch: naming a flattering competitor instead of the real alternative (the customer's true alternative is usually a spreadsheet or inertia); choosing a category because it's trendy ("AI platform") rather than because it makes your value obvious; defining best-fit customers by firmographics instead of by who cares most about the value; and stating value as adjectives ("powerful, intuitive") instead of provable outcomes.
