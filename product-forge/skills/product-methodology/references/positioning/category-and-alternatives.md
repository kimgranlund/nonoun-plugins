# Category, competitive alternatives, and the Sales Pitch (April D.)

April D.'s two books form one argument across two surfaces. _Obviously Awesome_ (2019) is the positioning method — how you decide the context a product lives in (covered as a method in `dunford-positioning.md`). _Sales Pitch_ (2023) is the delivery — how you turn that positioning into a story a salesperson (or a landing page, or a founder) actually tells. This file focuses on the two ideas that bind them: **competitive alternatives** (the "do nothing," the spreadsheet, the intern) and **market category** as the strongest lever on perception — and then the **Sales Pitch structure** that operationalizes both. The throughline: positioning is the input; the sales pitch is the output; alternatives and category are the hinge.

## Competitive alternatives: what they'd do if you didn't exist

April D.'s most-skipped and most-load-bearing idea: your real competition is **whatever the customer would use or do if your product did not exist** — and that is rarely the vendor you fixate on. Get this wrong and everything downstream (your unique attributes, your value, your category) mis-resolves, because "unique" and "valuable" are only defined _relative to_ the alternative the customer is actually weighing. The alternatives teams forget, because they don't appear on a competitive grid:

- **Do nothing / the status quo.** The customer keeps living with the problem. For genuinely new products this is the _dominant_ alternative — and the one teams never list, because "do nothing" has no logo. When the real alternative is inertia, your job is first to prove the problem is worth solving _at all_, which is a different pitch than out-featuring a rival.
- **The spreadsheet.** The generic stand-in for "they cobbled it together with tools they already own." If a customer's alternative is a spreadsheet, your differentiated value has to beat _free, familiar, and already-deployed_ — a high bar that features alone rarely clear.
- **The intern (hiring a person).** An intern, an analyst, an agency — the customer throws labor at the problem instead of buying software. The relevant comparison becomes cost, consistency, and scale, not feature parity with a competitor.

Naming alternatives honestly does two things at once: it surfaces the unique attributes that _actually_ matter (the ones a spreadsheet or an intern can't match), and it stops you positioning against a sophisticated rival on dimensions the customer isn't even weighing yet.

## Market category: the strongest single lever on perception

Customers reason by **analogy**: they decide what "kind of thing" your product is, and instantly import a bundle of assumptions — who it's for, what it competes with, what it should cost, what features are table-stakes. The market category you frame yourself into _triggers that bundle_. Choose the category that makes your unique value look obvious and your strengths look like the point; the wrong category silently imports the wrong comparisons and makes you look underfeatured or overpriced relative to a frame you never chose. Category is the highest-leverage component precisely because it sets the customer's entire expectation set in one word.

Two cautions April D. repeats. First, **don't pick a category because it's trendy** ("AI platform") — pick it because it makes your value obvious to best-fit customers. Second, framing into an _existing_ category (cheap, instant context) is different from _designing a new_ category (expensive, slow, occasionally the only honest option); most teams should frame into a category customers already understand rather than evangelize a new one.

## The Sales Pitch structure: Setup, then Follow-through

_Sales Pitch_'s thesis is that the conventional pitch — **company slide, problem slide, product walkthrough** — fails because it never gives the buyer a way to _think about the market_, and buries (or omits) what's actually unique. April D. replaces it with a structure in two acts. The **Setup** gives the customer a way to think about the whole market and aligns them with your point of view; the **Follow-through** is your differentiated value and how you deliver it.

```text
SETUP  (frame the market; earn the right to be different)
  1. Insight       — the critical thing best-fit customers must believe for your
                     differentiated value to matter. The point of view on the market.
                     (Help Scout's: "customer service is a growth driver, not a cost center.")
  2. Alternatives  — the honest menu of approaches to the problem, grouped into a few
                     "approaches," with real pros and cons — INCLUDING your own. The rep
                     acts as a guide, not a disparager. (This IS the positioning's
                     competitive alternatives, brought on stage.)
  3. Perfect world — the criteria for an ideal solution, derived from the insight +
                     alternatives. ("An easy-to-use tool that ALSO has advanced features.")
                     These criteria are rigged, honestly, to foreshadow your strengths.

FOLLOW-THROUGH  (now, and only now, the product)
  4. Introduce the product against the "perfect world" criteria you just established.
  5. Differentiated value — map each unique capability to a customer outcome ("so what?"),
     anchored back to the insight. This is the core; everything before earned its hearing.
  6. Proof — facts, demos, third-party validation. Value must be provable, not adjectival.
  7. The ask — a clear next action.
```

The discipline is the sequence. You **do not show the product until step 4**, after the buyer has a frame (insight), an honest map of their options (alternatives), and a yardstick (perfect world). By then your differentiated value lands as the obvious answer to criteria the buyer now holds — instead of a feature dump they have no way to weigh.

## How it chains back to positioning

The Sales Pitch is positioning made sayable. The mapping is exact:

```text
Positioning component (Obviously Awesome)   ->  Sales Pitch element (Sales Pitch)
------------------------------------------      --------------------------------
Competitive alternatives                    ->  Alternatives (the honest menu)
Unique attributes + Value (provable)        ->  Differentiated value + Proof
Best-fit customers                          ->  who the Insight is aimed at
Market category                             ->  the frame the Insight + Alternatives set
(the team's point of view)                  ->  Insight
```

If the pitch feels weak, the fix is usually upstream in the positioning, not in the slides: a vague Insight means no chosen point of view; an Alternatives section that lists only logo'd competitors means the team never took "do nothing / the spreadsheet" seriously; differentiated value stated as adjectives ("powerful, intuitive") means the value was never made provable.

## The "frame, alternatives, then value" test

A fast check that a pitch is built on chosen positioning, not a default product tour:

```text
Walk the pitch and confirm each is present, in order:

  - Insight first?  Before any product, is there ONE thing the buyer must believe —
    a point of view on the market — that makes your value matter? If the pitch opens
    on a company/problem/product slide, you have a product tour, not a setup.
  - Honest alternatives?  Is there a real menu of approaches, with pros AND cons,
    including "do nothing", the spreadsheet, the intern — and your own approach
    weighed fairly? A pitch that ignores or only trashes competitors isn't a guide.
  - Criteria before product?  Are the "ideal solution" criteria set BEFORE the demo,
    so the product arrives as the answer to a yardstick the buyer already holds?
  - Provable value, mapped to the insight?  Is each capability tied to an outcome and
    to the opening insight — and backed by proof, not adjectives?
  - Category check?  Hearing your frame, would a best-fit customer import expectations
    that FLATTER your strengths? If the frame makes you look underfeatured or
    overpriced, the category is wrong — choose another.
```

Failure modes to watch: opening on the product instead of the insight (the classic walkthrough); an alternatives slide that names only the rival you like to beat (the customer's true alternative is usually inertia or a spreadsheet); "perfect world" criteria invented _after_ the demo (so they read as self-serving rather than as a shared yardstick); and value asserted in adjectives the buyer can't verify.
