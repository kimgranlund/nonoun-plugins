# The Product Reframe

This is the product adaptation of the **Reframe** archetype from the global `plan-vision` skill — a peer authoring method, restated here for arguing that a product space, category, or competitive frame should be seen differently than it commonly is. A reframe is surgical, not sprawling. It does not propose a whole product the way a manifesto does; it changes the lens through which a team looks at one — what the product is competing on, who the real buyer is, what category it actually plays in, what the unit of value really is. The thesis is a lens, not a system.

> The highest-leverage moment in a product reframe is when the reader realizes the team has been answering the wrong question. "How do we beat competitor X on features" becomes "we are not in a feature race, we are in a switching-cost race." "How do we improve onboarding" becomes "users do not have an onboarding problem, they have a 'why should I trust this' problem." The reframe is not a rhetorical trick — it is a moment of first-principles clarity that re-aims the entire effort. A reframe that lands is worth more than a manifesto that sprawls.

## When to use it

Reach for the reframe when there is a **widespread received wisdom** about a product space that you believe is a misdiagnosis, and correcting it would change where the team pushes. It is the right form when the team already agrees on the goal but is, in your read, optimizing the wrong variable — racing on price when the real lever is positioning, chasing acquisition when the real leak is retention, copying a competitor's feature set when the moat is somewhere the features cannot reach. It is the wrong form when the team's frame is already correct (forcing a reframe produces a straw-man opening) or when you are proposing an entire direction (that is a manifesto). Target ~1500–2500 words: narrow, dense, one idea pursued to the end.

## The structure

A reframe is built around one pivot and a handful of self-contained leverage points.

1. **The current view vs. the reframed view.** A two-paragraph opening: first paragraph states cleanly how the space is conventionally seen — generously, not as a straw man; second paragraph delivers the reframe. Close on a short paragraph that states the new thesis cleanly, then a blockquoted one-liner of the reframe — the memo's core claim in a single sentence.
2. **The root problem / root misdiagnosis.** One section establishing _why_ the conventional view is not just incomplete but actively misleading — why the reframe is necessary, not merely interesting. This is the "failure to face the obstacle" diagnosis: name the specific structural reality the common frame skates past.
3. **The N leverage points (5–7).** The body. Each numbered sub-section explores one consequence of seeing the space the new way. Each opens with a sentence stating the claim, develops it with mechanics (a retention curve, a unit-economics identity, a network effect, a buyer-incentive structure), shows a concrete case, and closes with a bolded **The leverage:** line — one paragraph on what seeing it this way actually buys you. The bolded leverage line is the genre's signature; every leverage point must stand alone, so a reader who reads only one still gets value.
4. **The meta-point — why this matters more now / at scale / in this category.** The elevation. Why the reframe is more consequential than it first appears — often by contrasting with a space where the old frame _is_ fine, to show why the stakes are different here.

Close with a **distillation table** (leverage point → what it does → why it is different here) and a closing line built as three short parallel sentences, the third being the twist.

## The voice discipline

**Thesis-first, and the pivot must be crisp.** Show the old view cleanly, then replace it in one move; a muddy contrast kills the whole memo. **Opinionated.** The reframe is asserted as correct, not floated as a perspective; you are not running a debate between the two views, you are retiring one. **Physics-literal.** A product reframe earns its authority from mechanics the reader can check: if you claim "this is a switching-cost game, not a feature game," show the switching-cost math (what it costs a user to leave, and why that number, not the feature delta, predicts retention) — otherwise it is just a vibe and should be cut. **Dense — every paragraph earns its place.** A reframe is the shortest of the vision forms; there is no room for a paragraph that could appear in any memo. The closing should sound like a mic drop: short, declarative, memorable.

## A short worked product example

**Space:** a B2B SaaS team losing a feature-by-feature comparison war against a better-funded competitor.

**Current view vs. reframed view:** _The common frame:_ "We are behind on features; we need to close the gap on the comparison grid buyers use." _The reframe:_ we are not losing a feature race — we are losing a **default-tool race**, and on that axis the comparison grid is nearly irrelevant. Buyers do not pick the longest feature list; they pick the tool their team already lives in and would resent leaving.

**Blockquoted thesis:** _The competitor is not winning on features. They are winning on already-being-installed. Feature parity is a tax we are paying to fight on the wrong field._

**Root misdiagnosis:** the feature-gap frame treats the purchase as a rational spec comparison. It is not. The structural reality it skates past is that, post-trial, the deciding variable is **switching cost from the incumbent tool**, and a feature the buyer will not migrate to is a feature that does not move the deal.

**A leverage point (one of six):** _Switching cost beats feature count._ Mechanics: a buyer's probability of switching is roughly inverse to the integration depth and habit-stock they have built — not to the feature delta. A case: two near-identical losses where we had _more_ features but the team had three years of saved workflows in the incumbent. **The leverage:** stop spending the roadmap closing grid gaps; spend it lowering _our_ switching cost (import everything in one click) and, post-sale, raising it (make us the tool their workflows live in). The same roadmap dollars buy retention instead of a checkbox no one migrates for.

**Meta-point:** in a young, undifferentiated category, the feature grid genuinely does decide deals — which is why the old frame was once right and is now a trap. The category matured; switching cost became the dominant term in the equation, and the team kept optimizing the term that stopped mattering. That is why this reframe matters _now_ and would not have a year ago.

Note how the form does the work: one clean pivot, leverage points each defensible on their own mechanics, and a meta-point that explains why a once-correct frame became a liability — which is exactly the kind of misdiagnosis a reframe exists to catch.
