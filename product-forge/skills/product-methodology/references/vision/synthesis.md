# The Product Synthesis

This is the product adaptation of the **Synthesis** archetype from the global `plan-vision` skill — a peer authoring method, restated here for the situation where two or more competing product visions exist and someone has to resolve them. A synthesis is analysis-driven, not declarative: it maps where the positions genuinely agree, isolates the real conflicts from the merely apparent ones, names the philosophical gaps each leaves the other, and then — having been fair — takes a position. It is the form for "growth-led vs. brand-led," "platform vs. application," "self-serve vs. sales-led," "ship-fast vs. get-it-right" — any moment two camps each hold part of the truth and the org is paying for the unresolved fight.

> A synthesis is fair until it is time to choose. The discipline that separates a synthesis from a mush-the-middle compromise is that it **names the genuine conflicts as forced choices and pays the cost of resolving them** — it does not pretend both camps can have everything. The convergences show the positions are tracking something real; the conflicts show where a decision is unavoidable; the net assessment is the earned recommendation. A synthesis that resolves nothing is just a literature review of an argument.

## When to use it

Reach for the synthesis when there are two (or more) coherent, defended product positions and the cost of the unresolved standoff is real — teams pulling against each other, a strategy that oscillates between camps each planning cycle, a leadership team that keeps relitigating the same fight. It is the right form when both sides are smart and partly right (if one side is simply wrong, write a reframe and retire it). It is the wrong form when there is no genuine contest, or when you are proposing a single new direction unprompted by an existing argument (a manifesto). Target ~1500–3000 words. The test of whether you need a synthesis: can you state each position so well its proponent would nod? If not, you are not ready to resolve them.

## The structure

Five movements: frame, converge, contest, fill the gaps, then decide.

1. **Context.** Brief framing — what the two positions are, who holds them, and why the comparison matters _now_ (what the unresolved fight is costing). Two or three paragraphs, no more.
2. **Deep alignments.** What both positions actually agree on, stated as bolded points with a sentence each. Convergence is evidence: if two serious camps independently land on the same fundamentals, those fundamentals are probably real and should be treated as settled, not re-argued. Usually 3–5.
3. **The genuine conflicts.** The core. For each real conflict, a four-beat block: **Position A** (what it says), **Position B** (what it says), **Why this matters** (the concrete consequence of each choice — the failure mode each produces, not an abstract trade-off), and **Synthesis path** (how, or whether, the two reconcile). Usually 2–4 conflicts. The "why this matters" beat is non-negotiable — without it the reader gets a tidy tension and no stakes.
4. **The philosophical gaps.** Two short symmetric sections: what Position A has that B lacks, and what B has that A lacks. This is where you credit each side with the thing the other is blind to — the gaps are why neither position alone is sufficient.
5. **Net assessment.** The synthesis. The integrated approach, stated as 3–5 numbered recommendations, each with its reasoning. Then a closing paragraph that names the **biggest risk for each original position** and why the combination mitigates it — proof the synthesis has thought about what could go wrong with each camp, not just split the difference.

## The voice discipline

**Evenhanded in the analysis, then opinionated in the assessment.** This is the one vision form that is genuinely fair early — but it is not neutral at the end; the net assessment takes a side, and a synthesis that refuses to is a survey. **Thesis-first within the resolution.** The net assessment should lead with the integrated position, not bury it under restated trade-offs. **Physics-literal in the conflicts.** "Why this matters" must name a real mechanism — what each choice actually produces (the failure mode, the cost curve, the incentive it creates) — not a hand-waved tension. **Dense — every paragraph earns its place.** The synthesis must feel _earned_ by the analysis, not imposed on it; the fastest way to lose the reader is a recommendation that does not follow from the conflicts you just mapped. Close on the risk framing, because a synthesis that has not reckoned with its own failure modes has not finished thinking.

## A short worked product example

**Positions:** **Growth-led** (instrument everything, optimize the funnel, let metrics decide the roadmap) vs. **Brand/craft-led** (protect the product's coherence and reputation, refuse metric-chasing that cheapens the experience). The fight is costing the org a roadmap that lurches between A/B-test wins and craft rewrites each cycle.

**Deep alignments:** **Both want durable growth** — neither is arguing for a worse business. **Both agree the customer experience is the engine** — they disagree on how to read it, not whether it matters. **Both distrust vanity metrics** — growth-led wants _causal_ metrics, brand-led wants metrics that do not corrode trust; the shared enemy is the metric that flatters without meaning.

**A genuine conflict (one of three):** _What decides the roadmap?_ **Growth-led:** the experiment with the highest measured lift wins. **Brand/craft-led:** the bet that protects long-term coherence wins, even when it loses the short-term test. **Why this matters:** pure growth-led produces local maxima — a funnel of individually-optimized steps that add up to an incoherent product users do not love (and quietly churn from past the measurement window); pure craft-led produces beautiful products that cannot prove they are working and starve for resources. **Synthesis path:** let experiments decide _within_ a coherence envelope the craft side owns — growth optimizes freely inside guardrails that protect the things measurement cannot see on a two-week horizon.

**Gaps growth-led fills for craft-led:** falsifiability — a way to know a craft bet actually worked rather than merely feeling right. **Gaps craft-led fills for growth-led:** the long horizon — protection against optimizing the measurable at the expense of the durable, which the funnel cannot see.

**Net assessment:** (1) **Adopt a coherence envelope** — craft owns a small set of non-negotiable experience invariants; everything else is fair game for experimentation. (2) **Require a causal metric _and_ a coherence check on every bet** — a bet must move a real number and not breach an invariant. (3) **Lengthen the measurement window on craft bets** — judge retention/trust over cohorts, not clicks over days, so the long-horizon value becomes measurable instead of merely asserted. _Closing risk framing:_ the growth position's worst failure is a high-converting product no one is loyal to; the craft position's worst failure is an unfalsifiable one no one can fund. The envelope-plus-causal-metric synthesis is more robust than either alone because it makes the craft side falsifiable and the growth side accountable to the long horizon — each camp's blind spot is exactly the other's discipline.

Note how the form does the work: it credits both camps with something real, isolates the one decision that actually forks (what decides the roadmap), prices each pure position's failure mode, and lands a recommendation that is visibly _earned_ by the conflict rather than a split-the-difference truce.
