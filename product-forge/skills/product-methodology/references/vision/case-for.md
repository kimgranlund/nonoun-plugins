# The Product Case-For

This is the product adaptation of the **Case-For** archetype from the global `plan-vision` skill — a peer authoring method, restated here for making the business and strategic argument for a product approach, bet, or investment. Where a manifesto argues a whole direction and a reframe re-aims a frame, a case-for is built for decision-makers who must decide whether an approach is the right investment _right now_. It answers not only what the approach is, but why it pays back, why this is the moment, and how to adopt it without betting the company. The audience is someone holding a budget and an opportunity cost.

> The case-for's signature hook is the **misdiagnosis**: open with the problem the org has been ignoring or mis-solving, show the conventional response, then pivot to why that response cannot work and what the problem actually is. The reframe earns the reader's attention; the "why now" earns their urgency; the transition path earns their yes. A case-for that skips the misdiagnosis is a pitch; a pitch argues a conclusion, a case-for argues its way to one.

## When to use it

Reach for the case-for when you need a decision and the decider needs to understand the _investment_, not just the idea — to fund a bet, to reprioritize a portfolio, to commit a team to an approach with real opportunity cost. It is the right form when the argument is fundamentally economic or strategic ("this is worth doing now, here is why it pays back, here is how we phase the risk") rather than purely conceptual. It is the wrong form when there is no decision on the table (that is a reframe or a think piece) or when you are laying out an entire product (a manifesto). Target ~2000–3500 words. The thing that distinguishes a case-for from a reframe is the **transition path**: the pragmatic adoption sequence is part of the argument, not an appendix.

## The structure

Eight movements. The first three set up the problem and the principles; the middle four make and price the case; the last elevates it.

1. **The problem we've been ignoring / misdiagnosing.** Open with the common, wrong diagnosis and the conventional response it produces. Then the pivot paragraph: what the problem actually is. Close on a short italicized line — the reframe in one sentence.
2. **Why it matters now — the inflection.** The "why now." What specifically _changed_ — a buyer shift, a platform shift, a cost inversion, a scaling dynamic, a new enabling technology — that makes this urgent rather than evergreen. If the same argument was equally true three years ago, you have not found the real inflection; keep digging. End on a bolded summary of the shift in consequences.
3. **First principles — what this actually is.** Strip to atoms, tighter than a manifesto's reduction. One bolded irreducible truth per paragraph, usually three. These are the premises the rest of the case stands on.
4. **The approach.** What the reframed view produces as a solution — one paragraph on what the approach _is_ — followed by **what it gives you**: 4–6 bolded benefits, each a consequence that follows from the first principles, not a feature wish.
5. **What changes in practice.** The concrete implications, usually subdivided by role/team (what changes for product, for engineering, for go-to-market). This grounds the abstraction in someone's altered Monday.
6. **The scaling argument.** Why the approach becomes _more_ valuable as the company grows — the place to argue that a structural guarantee beats a process guarantee, and that "later" quietly becomes "never" if you defer.
7. **The economic / compounding argument.** The payback reasoning. For product audiences this is usually time-to-learning, retention compounding, CAC/LTV movement, or avoided rework — stated as a mechanism, not a hand-wave.
8. **The transition.** The pragmatic adoption path, usually three demand-driven stages: a low-risk first step, a backfill/expansion, then the default state. This is the part that converts a sympathetic reader into a committed one.

Close with **the deeper point** — why this matters beyond the immediate decision, ideally a principle the reader can carry to other bets — and a closing line that states that principle as an asymmetry or a constraint.

## The voice discipline

**Thesis-first, and the misdiagnosis must be sharp.** The opening reframe is the hook; if it is soft, the decider reads on as a skeptic. **Opinionated, not balanced.** A case-for takes the position that the approach is right and the alternative is a false economy; it does not lay out pros and cons evenhandedly — that is a memo for a different skill. **Physics-literal in the economics.** "It pays back" is a claim you must be able to compute: name the mechanism (rework avoided per cycle, retention points per cohort, learning velocity per quarter) so the payback is a number's shadow, not a slogan. **Dense — every paragraph earns its place.** A case-for read by a busy decider cannot afford a paragraph that does not advance the decision. The "why now" must be specific and the transition must be real; those two sections are where weak cases collapse.

## A short worked product example

**Approach:** moving a product org from a feature-roadmap operating model to **continuous discovery** (weekly customer touch, assumption-testing before build).

**The problem we've been ignoring:** _Common diagnosis:_ "we ship too slowly; we need to execute the roadmap faster." _Conventional response:_ more sprints, tighter tickets, a velocity dashboard. _The pivot:_ the org does not have a speed problem, it has a **direction problem** — it ships fast in directions that do not move outcomes, then blames execution. _Italicized line:_ "We are not slow. We are precise about building the wrong things."

**Why now:** the inflection is that the product has reached the stage where the cost of a wrong bet is no longer one team's quarter — it is a market window. Early on, building the wrong thing was cheap tuition; at current scale, a mis-aimed quarter is a competitor's opening. **The shift: wrong-direction work went from affordable to existential.**

**First principles (one of three):** _Build effort is irreversible; learning is cheap._ A shipped feature is a sunk commitment with a maintenance tail; a tested assumption costs a week and can be thrown away for free. An org that learns before it builds is trading a cheap, reversible cost for an expensive, irreversible one.

**The approach / what it gives you:** continuous discovery makes assumption-testing a weekly habit upstream of build. _Benefit (one of five):_ **Wrong bets die in a week, not a quarter** — the org's unit of waste drops from a shipped feature to a discarded interview.

**What changes in practice — for product:** the PM's week reorganizes around customer contact and assumption maps, not backlog grooming. **For engineering:** fewer, better-aimed builds; prototypes for learning are explicitly disposable. **For go-to-market:** positioning is informed by what customers actually said, not what was shipped.

**Scaling argument:** the value compounds with team count — at one team, ad-hoc discovery is survivable; at ten teams, the only thing that keeps ten roadmaps aimed at real outcomes is a structural discovery habit, because process exhortation does not scale and structure does.

**Economic argument:** the payback is avoided rework. If discovery kills even one mis-aimed quarter per team per year, the week-per-cycle cost is dwarfed by the quarter-per-team it saves — a mechanism, not a hope.

**The transition:** Stage 1 — one team, three customer conversations a week, no process mandate. Stage 2 — backfill the teams that ask for it after they see the first team's hit rate. Stage 3 — discovery is simply how product works here, defended structurally, not by reminder.

**The deeper point:** the asymmetry between cheap, reversible learning and expensive, irreversible building is not specific to discovery — it is the constraint behind every good product decision, and a team that internalizes it will make better bets long after this particular approach has a different name.

Note how the form does the work: the misdiagnosis reframes "slow" as "mis-aimed," the "why now" is genuinely time-bound (it would not have held early on), and the staged, demand-driven transition lets a cautious decider say yes to one team before betting the org.
