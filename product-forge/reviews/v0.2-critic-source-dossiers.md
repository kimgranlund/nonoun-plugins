# product-forge v0.2 — critic source dossiers

Method and anti-fabrication standard: this file assembles sourcing for six proposed council critics, all of whom are **living people**. It was built with web search plus direct fetches of primary sources (the critics' own books, sites, essays, talks, and posts), preferring primary over third-party summaries. Every candidate quote below is reproduced as published, in quotation marks, with the exact source URL or book + location and one line of context. A quote appears **only** when its precise public source was located; where a primary PDF/site could not be machine-read, that is stated. Nothing here is paraphrase-dressed-as-quote, and no fragments were stitched into invented sentences. The highest-priority failure mode is a fabricated or misattributed quote — so where a famous line turned out to belong to someone else (e.g. the "two coffee shops" line is **not** Marc S.'s), that is flagged explicitly rather than attributed for convenience. Quote counts and verification gaps are summarized per critic at the end of each section and in the closing manifest.

## 1. Jesse G. — experience architecture (the five planes)

**Verifiable identity.** Jesse G.. Co-founder of the UX consultancy Adaptive Path; author of _The Elements of User Experience_; coined the term "Ajax" (Feb 2005). His own current site describes him as "Executive design leadership coach, author of The Elements of User Experience, and co-founder of Adaptive Path" and headlines his present focus as "AI transformation for design leadership, author, and speaker." Source: <https://jessejamesgarrett.com/about/> and <https://jessejamesgarrett.com/>. Historical title at time of the Ajax essay: "President and a founder of Adaptive Path" (stated in the 2005 essay byline below). Best-sourced current role is therefore "executive design leadership coach / AI-transformation advisor for design leadership" per his own site; the older "President, Adaptive Path" title is historical (Adaptive Path was later acquired by Capital One).

**Core lens / framework.**

- The **five planes** model — Strategy → Scope → Structure → Skeleton → Surface — a layered framework for user-centered design where each plane depends on the ones below it. Source: _The Elements of User Experience: User-Centered Design for the Web_, Jesse G., New Riders, 2002 (2nd ed. 2010, subtitled "...for the Web and Beyond"). Canonical diagram and site: <http://www.jjg.net/elements/> (note: jjg.net is HTTP-only and refused connection during research; identity/framework corroborated via his current site and the 2005 essay, both fetched successfully).
- **Experience as a deliberately designed gap to close** — the difference between what an interface can provide and what users actually get; UX as the thing you build up plane by plane rather than decorate at the end.
- **Ajax / interaction responsiveness** — naming and articulating the asynchronous web-application interaction model so that interfaces stop making users wait. Source: "Ajax: A New Approach to Web Applications," Adaptive Path, Feb 18 2005 (full text fetched; quotes below).

**Candidate verbatim quotes** (all from the 2005 Ajax essay, fetched in full as a primary-source PDF mirror of the Adaptive Path original; byline "by Jesse G., February 18, 2005"):

1. "This approach makes a lot of technical sense, but it doesn't make for a great user experience. While the server is doing its thing, what's the user doing? That's right, waiting. And at every step in a task, the user waits some more." — Context: Garrett diagnosing why the classic request/response web model fails users. Source: _Ajax: A New Approach to Web Applications_, section "Defining Ajax" / Figure 1 caption. Mirror fetched: <https://immagic.com/eLibrary/ARCHIVES/GENERAL/ADTVPATH/A050218G.pdf> (original archived at adaptivepath.com/publications/essays/archives/000385.php).
2. "We must be careful to use Ajax to enhance the user experience of our applications, not degrade it." — Context: his closing Q&A answer warning that more interaction power demands more design caution. Source: same essay, "Ajax Q&A," answer to "Do Ajax applications always deliver a better experience than traditional web applications?"
3. "I needed something shorter than 'Asynchronous JavaScript+CSS+DOM+XMLHttpRequest' to use when discussing this approach with clients." — Context: his own first-person account of why he coined the name "Ajax." Source: same essay, "Ajax Q&A," answer to "Why did you feel the need to give this a name?"
4. "The biggest challenges in creating Ajax applications are not technical... Instead, the challenges are for the designers of these applications: to forget what we think we know about the limitations of the Web, and begin to imagine a wider, richer range of possibilities." — Context: his "Moving Forward" section, framing the problem as a design problem, not an engineering one. Source: same essay, section "Moving Forward." (Ellipsis marks an omitted clause — "The core Ajax technologies are mature, stable, and well understood." — and is shown so the quote is not presented as one unbroken sentence.)

First-person identity quote (from his own site, useful for framing his lens): "I started out in user experience before it even had that name — in fact, some would say I'm one of the people who helped give it that name." Source: <https://jessejamesgarrett.com/about/>.

**What he attacks in a product artifact.**

- "Which plane is this decision actually on?" — calling out scope/structure/surface confusion (e.g. arguing about button color before strategy and scope are settled).
- "What's the user doing while the system does its thing?" — latency, waiting states, and the start-stop-start rhythm of an interaction.
- "Does the surface depend on a structure that depends on a defined strategy — or is this skin over a void?" — top-down decoration with no strategy plane underneath.
- "You have more interaction power here; where's the matching caution?" — richness that degrades rather than enhances the experience.
- "Is this good for software, or are you reusing a model that was only ever good for hypertext/documents?" — applying document-era patterns to application problems.

**Sourcing caveat.** The five-planes framing is paraphrased from the book's well-known model, not quoted, because the canonical jjg.net pages and the chapter-2 PDF are HTTP-only and refused connection (ECONNREFUSED) during research; all four verbatim quotes are from the 2005 Ajax essay, whose full text was machine-read and is unambiguously his. Current title is taken from his own live site and is best-sourced as "executive design leadership coach / AI-transformation advisor"; the "President, Adaptive Path" title is historical. No quote was sourced directly from _The Elements of User Experience_ itself (book not machine-readable here) — if a book quote is wanted for the critic file, it should be pulled from a physical/edition copy and page-cited before use.

## 2. Abby C. — information architecture / sensemaking

**Verifiable identity.** Abby C.. Self-described "Information Architect"; author of _How to Make Sense of Any Mess: Information Architecture for Everybody_ (self-published, 2014). She is a past president of the IA Institute and a longtime IA educator. Sources: <https://abbycovert.com/> (site header / identity), book site <https://www.howtomakesenseofanymess.com/>. Affiliation note: she has been associated with The Understanding Group (TUG) (<https://understandinggroup.com/>); her own site presents her independently as "Abby C., Information Architect," which is the safest current descriptor.

**Core lens / framework.**

- **Information architecture as sensemaking for everybody** — IA is not a specialist deliverable but a shared human responsibility to arrange things so they're understandable. Source: _How to Make Sense of Any Mess_, 2014.
- **The object/practice definition of IA** — IA as both a thing you make and an act of deciding arrangement to convey intended meaning. Source: book glossary, <http://www.howtomakesenseofanymess.com/term/noun/information-architecture/> (fetched).
- **Mental models and shared language** — messes are largely language and meaning problems; the map in your head isn't visible to others. Source: book, chapter on language/meaning.
- **Deciding what something _won't_ be** — clarity comes as much from exclusion as inclusion. Source: book.

**Candidate verbatim quotes.**

1. (Definition, the canonical one) "As an object: The way we arrange the parts of something to make it more understandable as a whole. As a practice: The act of deciding which order the pieces of a whole should be arranged in order to communicate the meaning that is intended to users." — Context: her formal two-part definition of "information architecture." Source: official book glossary, <http://www.howtomakesenseofanymess.com/term/noun/information-architecture/> (fetched verbatim). NOTE: the widely circulated short form drops one word — "the way we arrange the parts of something to make it understandable" — the exact glossary text reads "make it **more** understandable as a whole." Use the glossary wording.
2. "When we determine what something won't be, we often reveal a little more about what it will be." — Context: on the clarifying power of exclusion/constraint. Source: _How to Make Sense of Any Mess_; reproduced on the book's Goodreads quotations page, <https://www.goodreads.com/work/quotes/43156580>.
3. "When you discuss a specific subject, you subconsciously reference part of a large internal map of what you know. Other people can't see this map. It only exists in your head, and it's called your mental model." — Context: introducing mental models as the root of misalignment. Source: book; Goodreads quotations page (same URL as above).
4. "Practicing information architecture means exhibiting the courage to push past the edges of your current reality. It means asking questions that inspire change. It takes honesty and confidence in other people." — Context: on IA as change work that requires nerve, not just diagrams. Source: book; Goodreads quotations page (same URL).
5. (First-person, from her own essay) "Without dealing with other people, we are not practicing IA, we are drawing pictures of structures that will likely never be built." — Context: arguing IA is interpersonal/political, not just diagramming. Source: Abby C., "Information is made of people," <https://abbycovert.com/writing/information-is-made-of-people/> (fetched).

**What she attacks in a product artifact.**

- "Whose mental model is this organized around — yours, or the user's?" — structure that mirrors the org chart or the database instead of the user's understanding.
- "What are you deciding this _won't_ be?" — scope and taxonomy that never says no, so nothing is actually understandable.
- "What do these words mean, and does everyone in the room mean the same thing by them?" — undefined, overloaded, or internally-jargon labels.
- "You've drawn a beautiful structure — who actually has to agree to build it, and have you done that interpersonal work?" — IA-as-diagram divorced from stakeholders and change management.
- "Is this arrangement making the whole more understandable, or just more complete?" — completeness mistaken for clarity.

**Sourcing caveat.** Quotes 2–4 are reproduced from the book via its Goodreads quotations page rather than a fetched copy of the book interior (the book site exposes only TOC/glossary, not full chapter text); they are widely and consistently attributed to this single book, and the glossary definition (quote 1) and the essay quote (quote 5) were fetched directly from her own domains, which raises overall confidence. If the critic file will print quotes 2–4, ideally confirm page numbers against a copy of the 2014 edition. The short-form definition variant is a real misquote risk — flagged above.

## 3. Torrey P. — content design

**Verifiable identity.** Torrey P.. Author of _Strategic Writing for UX_ (O'Reilly, 1st ed. June 2019; 2nd ed. 2025) — described as the first book on UX content design from a major publisher. Her own site gives her current role as **President of Catbird Content** ("helps teams solve business and customer problems using UX and content"). She has written UX content at Google, OfferUp, and Microsoft, and teaches at the University of Washington Information School. Sources: <https://www.torreypodmajersky.com/>, book page <https://www.torreypodmajersky.com/content-design-book>, publisher <https://www.oreilly.com/library/view/strategic-writing-for/9781492049388/>.

**Core lens / framework.**

- **UX content is strategic, not decoration** — words exist to meet user and organizational goals, not to be "fixed at the end." Source: _Strategic Writing for UX_, O'Reilly 2019.
- **Good UX text disappears** — content succeeds when users don't notice it, because they're focused on their task. Source: her interviews (verbatim below).
- **The voice chart** — a reusable artifact of "decision-making rules and creative guidance" that aligns content to product principles and to the people using the experience. Source: _Strategic Writing for UX_, voice-chart chapter ("Lift Every Voice").
- **Content-first / "everything is content"** — even wordless layout carries content; the discipline is choosing the right content for the moment. Source: her talk/interview "Everything is Content."

**Candidate verbatim quotes.**

1. (First-person, primary interview) "UX text, for the most part, it should disappear from people's memory. It should just evaporate because they are there in this experience to accomplish a task or play a game or, or create their own wonderful writing or their own wonderful art." — Context: distinguishing UX writing from creative writing; the content shouldn't draw attention to itself. Source: "UX Writing: An interview with Torrey P.," UX Pod, <https://uxpod.com/episodes/ux-writing-an-interview-with-torrey-podmajersky.html> (fetched; attributed to Torrey, not the host). The mid-sentence "or, or" repetition is preserved verbatim from the spoken transcript.
2. (First-person, primary interview) "The valuable part of what we do is the decision making, is the what is the right content for this moment? What are all the possibilities and which one might work better?" — Context: locating the value of content design in judgment, not wordsmithing. Source: "Torrey P.: Everything is Content," Content Strategy Insights (ellessmedia), <https://ellessmedia.com/csi/torrey-2/> (fetched; spoken transcript, verbatim including its run-on grammar).
3. (Book) "Before the writing can begin, the writer needs to identify the goals of the person who will use the experience as well as the goals of the organization making the experience." — Context: the dual-goal premise of strategic UX writing. Source: _Strategic Writing for UX_; reproduced on the book's Goodreads quotations page, <https://www.goodreads.com/work/quotes/68652898>.
4. (Book) "While the organization attracts, converts, onboards, engages, supports, and transforms, the person investigates, verifies, commits to, sets up, uses, fixes, prefers, and champions the experience." — Context: her two-sided model of the org's funnel vs. the person's journey. Source: _Strategic Writing for UX_; Goodreads quotations page (same URL).

**What she attacks in a product artifact.**

- "Whose goals does this text serve — and can you name both the user's goal and the org's goal for this exact screen?" — content with no strategy behind the words.
- "Were the words designed, or written at the end to fill the boxes?" — UX writing treated as a finishing/cosmetic step.
- "What's your voice chart — what decision rules make this content consistent and on-principle?" — voice asserted as vibes, not codified.
- "Does this content disappear, or does it make the user stop and parse it?" — cleverness/branding that taxes the user mid-task.
- "Error, empty, and edge states: where's the content for when the experience breaks?" — happy-path-only copy.

**Sourcing caveat.** Two strong first-person quotes are from spoken interview transcripts on third-party sites (UX Pod; ellessmedia/Content Strategy Insights) — they are clearly attributed to Torrey, but each is effectively single-source for its exact wording and transcript punctuation; treat the verbatim spoken grammar as authentic-but-oral. The two book quotes come via Goodreads rather than a fetched book interior; confirm page numbers against the 2019 (or note 2025 2nd) edition if printed. Her current title (President, Catbird Content) is from her own site and is well-sourced; some third-party bios still list her as a Google/Microsoft UX writer (historical roles).

## 4. Ann C. — privacy / trust / safety (Privacy by Design)

**Verifiable identity.** Ann C., Ph.D. Originator of Privacy by Design. The canonical document bylines her as "Ann C., Ph.D., Information & Privacy Commissioner, Ontario, Canada" — her role at the time the framework was codified. She served three terms as Information & Privacy Commissioner of Ontario (2017 era and later: founder/executive roles at privacy-by-design centres). Source (primary, fetched in full): "Privacy by Design — The 7 Foundational Principles," Ann C., Office of the Information & Privacy Commissioner of Ontario (archival copy at SFU: <https://www.sfu.ca/~palys/Ann C.-2011-PrivacyByDesign-7FoundationalPrinciples.pdf>; also at <https://www.privacybydesign.ca>). Best-sourced current role: founder/leader of privacy-by-design advisory work (e.g. Global Privacy & Security by Design); the "Information & Privacy Commissioner, Ontario" title in the quotes below is **historical** (her title as document author), not her present-day office.

**Core lens / framework — the 7 Foundational Principles** (titles reproduced exactly from the primary document; each followed by its exact opening descriptive sentence, all verbatim from the fetched PDF):

1. **Proactive not Reactive; Preventative not Remedial** — "The Privacy by Design approach is characterized by proactive rather than reactive measures."
2. **Privacy as the Default** — "We can all be certain of one thing — the default rules!"
3. **Privacy Embedded into Design** — "Privacy by Design is embedded into the design and architecture of IT systems and business practices."
4. **Full Functionality — Positive-Sum, not Zero-Sum** — "Privacy by Design seeks to accommodate all legitimate interests and objectives in a positive-sum 'win-win' manner, not through a dated, zero-sum approach, where unnecessary trade-offs are made."
5. **End-to-End Security — Lifecycle Protection** — "Privacy by Design, having been embedded into the system prior to the first element of information being collected, extends securely throughout the entire lifecycle of the data involved — strong security measures are essential to privacy, from start to finish."
6. **Visibility and Transparency** — "Privacy by Design seeks to assure all stakeholders that whatever the business practice or technology involved, it is in fact, operating according to the stated promises and objectives, subject to independent verification."
7. **Respect for User Privacy** — "Above all, Privacy by Design requires architects and operators to keep the interests of the individual uppermost by offering such measures as strong privacy defaults, appropriate notice, and empowering user-friendly options."

**Candidate verbatim quotes** (all reproduced exactly from the fetched primary PDF):

1. "In short, Privacy by Design comes before-the-fact, not after." — Context: closing line of Principle 1's description (proactive, not remedial). Source: 7 Foundational Principles, Principle 1.
2. "If an individual does nothing, their privacy still remains intact. No action is required on the part of the individual to protect their privacy — it is built into the system, by default." — Context: Principle 2, defining privacy-as-default. Source: same document, Principle 2.
3. "It is not bolted on as an add-on, after the fact. The result is that privacy becomes an essential component of the core functionality being delivered." — Context: Principle 3, privacy embedded in architecture. Source: same document, Principle 3.
4. "Privacy by Design avoids the pretence of false dichotomies, such as privacy vs. security, demonstrating that it is possible, and far more desirable, to have both." — Context: Principle 4, rejecting zero-sum trade-offs. Source: same document, Principle 4.
5. "The 'Security' principle has special relevance here because, at its essence, without strong security, there can be no privacy." — Context: Principle 5, security as a precondition for privacy. Source: same document, Principle 5. (Also note the memorable in-principle phrases "Remember, trust but verify!" (Principle 6) and "Keep it user-centric!" (Principle 7), both verbatim.)

**What she attacks in a product artifact.**

- "Is privacy designed in from the first element of data collected, or bolted on after launch?" — privacy as a remediation backlog rather than an architectural default.
- "What is the default if the user does nothing — is it the most privacy-protective state?" — opt-out dark patterns; privacy-hostile defaults.
- "Where's the false trade-off?" — any 'privacy vs. growth/security/personalization' framing presented as unavoidable (she rejects zero-sum).
- "Is data minimized — non-identifiable by default, retained only as long as necessary, then securely destroyed?" — over-collection and indefinite retention.
- "Can an outsider verify these promises? What's visible and transparent, and who is accountable by name?" — unverifiable privacy claims; no named owner; no redress mechanism.

**Sourcing caveat.** This is the strongest-sourced critic: all seven principle titles, their opening descriptions, and all five quotes were machine-read verbatim from the canonical primary PDF (the IPC Ontario document, SFU archival mirror). The only caveat is the title: the byline title "Information & Privacy Commissioner, Ontario, Canada" is her **historical** role as the document's author; her present-day affiliation should be described as privacy-by-design advisory/founder work rather than the Ontario commissioner's office (which she no longer holds). No quote here is single-source-risky — the document is widely mirrored with identical text.

## 5. Marc S. — service design

**Verifiable identity.** Marc S.. Lead author/editor of _This is Service Design Thinking_ (2011) and _This is Service Design Doing_ (O'Reilly, 2018), both co-authored (Doing: with Markus Edgar Hormess, Adam Lawrence, Jakob Schneider). Co-founder & CEO of **More than Metrics** (maker of the service-design tools Smaply and ExperienceFellow). Sources: book site <https://www.thisisservicedesigndoing.com/>; company <https://www.smaply.com/>; LinkedIn <https://at.linkedin.com/in/stickdorn> (title "Co-Founder & CEO, More Than Metrics"). Best-sourced current role: Co-Founder & CEO, More than Metrics / Smaply.

**Core lens / framework.**

- **The six principles of service design (revisited in _Doing_)** — human-centered, collaborative, iterative, sequential, real, holistic. (These updated the five principles from the 2011 _Thinking_ book: user-centered, co-creative, sequencing, evidencing, holistic.) Source: _This is Service Design Doing_, 2018; canonical wording reproduced on his company's site, <https://www.smaply.com/blog/service-design>.
- **Research-and-prototyping primacy** — if you do almost nothing else in service design, do research (verbatim quote below).
- **Co-creation, not design-for** — service design is done _with_ users and employees, never _for_ them (verbatim quote below).
- **Whole-journey orchestration** — services as sequences of interrelated touchpoints across the entire experience and across the business, not isolated screens.

**The six principles — exact wording** (reproduced verbatim from his company's site, which states the _This is Service Design Doing_ definitions):

1. **Human-centered** — "Consider the experience of all the people affected by the service."
2. **Collaborative** — "Stakeholders of various backgrounds and functions should be actively engaged in the service design process."
3. **Iterative** — "Service design is an exploratory, adaptive, and experimental approach, iterating toward implementation."
4. **Sequential** — "The service should be visualized and orchestrated as a sequence of interrelated actions."
5. **Real** — "Needs should be researched in reality, ideas prototyped in reality, and intangible values evidenced as physical or digital reality."
6. **Holistic** — "Services should sustainably address the needs of all stakeholders through the entire service and across business."

**Candidate verbatim quotes** (first-person Marc S., all from one primary Q&A he gave):

1. "When you only do two things in service design, do research and prototyping. When you only do one, do research." — Context: arguing qualitative research is the irreducible core of service design. Source: "Take the Service Design Challenge: Q&A with Marc S.," Genesys, <https://www.genesys.com/blog/post/take-the-service-design-challenge-qa-with-marc-stickdorn> (fetched; attributed to Marc S.).
2. "Service design cannot be done for the users of a service or the employees, it always needs to be done with users, customers and employees." — Context: the co-creation principle, in his own words. Source: same Genesys Q&A.
3. "It is often not that agents do not want to help customers, but it is the system that simply does not allow them to help customers." — Context: arguing service failures are systemic, not individual; start from the employee experience. Source: same Genesys Q&A.

**What he attacks in a product artifact.**

- "Where's the research with actual users and frontline employees — or are these assumptions?" — a PRD built on internal opinion, no qualitative evidence.
- "You've designed a screen; where's the rest of the journey — the touchpoints before, after, and backstage?" — front-stage UI with no service ecosystem (staff, systems, channels) behind it.
- "Was this prototyped in reality, or only specified?" — big-bang specs with no experiential prototyping.
- "Is this being done _with_ the people affected, or _for_ them?" — solutions handed down without co-creation.
- "When this breaks, is it the person's fault or the system's?" — designs that blame users/agents for what the system disallows.

**Sourcing caveat.** IMPORTANT anti-fabrication flag: the famous line often associated with this book — "When you have two coffee shops right next to each other... service design is what makes you walk into one and not the other" — is **NOT** Marc S.'s; the book attributes it to "31 Volts Service Design, 2008." Do not put it in Marc S.'s mouth. The three first-person quotes above all come from a **single** interview (Genesys Q&A), so they are single-source for exact wording — solid but worth a second corroboration before the critic file leans hard on any one of them. The six-principle definitions are quoted from his own company's site (primary-adjacent) and match the canonical _Doing_ text; ideally confirm against the 2018 book's "Principles, revisited" section for page citation. The books are multi-author, so a "principles" quote should be attributed to the book/author team, not to Marc S. personally.

## 6. John C. — governance & operating cadence (the product operating model)

**Verifiable identity.** John C.. Author of the widely-read product newsletter **The Beautiful Mess** (TBM) on Substack; formerly Product Evangelist / Head of Product Education at Amplitude; currently **Head of Product at Dotwork**. Sources: newsletter <https://cutlefish.substack.com/>; LinkedIn <https://www.linkedin.com/in/johnpcutler/> (current title "Head of Product @ Dotwork"); corroborated by 2025 conference billing ("John C., Head of Product, Dotwork," Mind the Product / INDUSTRY 2025, <https://www.mindtheproduct.com/a-better-approach-to-product-management-john-cutler/>). Best-sourced current role: Head of Product, Dotwork (ex-Amplitude).

**Core lens / framework.**

- **"The product operating model is not a destination"** — it's the capacity to keep adapting without losing coherence; copying its artifacts is a category error. Source: Cutler Substack note, 2024/25 (verbatim below).
- **Means–ends inversion** — teams mistake the _signals_ of a mature system (roles, rituals, artifacts) for the _conditions_ that produce maturity (judgment, learning, constraint, responsibility). Source: same note.
- **ProductOps as an internal platform product** — solving problems individual teams can't, with real customers (the cross-functional teams). Source: "TBM 45/52: What is ProductOps?" (verbatim below).
- **One roadmap / no hidden "technical" black box** — keeping all work visible on a single roadmap as a forcing function so foundational work isn't devalued. Source: "TBM 48/53: One Roadmap" (verbatim below).
- **"The beautiful mess"** — product development is irreducibly messy; resist over-clean models. (Thesis of the newsletter; framing, not a single quotable line.)

**Candidate verbatim quotes.**

1. "Here's why so many companies that are adopting the 'product operating model' will fail. Whenever a 'model' gets popular, you see a rush to break it down into structures, roles and responsibilities, competencies, assessments, etc." — Context: opening of his critique of cargo-culted operating models. Source: John C., Substack note, <https://substack.com/@cutlefish/note/c-192503059> (fetched; opening passage, contiguous).
2. "The 'product operating model' wasn't a destination. It was the ability to keep adapting without losing coherence." — Context: his core reframe in the same note. Source: same note (this two-sentence pair is contiguous; it is **not** adjacent to quote 1 above — listed separately to avoid stitching).
3. "This is a form of means–ends inversion, where the artifacts of a (theoretically) mature system are treated as the path to maturity itself." — Context: naming the failure mode — mistaking artifacts for the conditions that create them. Source: same note (also non-adjacent to the above; listed separately).
4. "ProductOps solves problems for product teams that single teams and individuals cannot solve." / "In this sense, ProductOps is an internal platform product. It is about collective leverage. Like any product, it requires product management and strategy." — Context: his working definition of ProductOps and the platform-product framing. Source: "TBM 45/52: What is ProductOps?", <https://cutlefish.substack.com/p/tbm-4552-what-is-productops> (fetched; the two segments are from different points in the piece — separated by " / " and not presented as one sentence).
5. "But no one knows that because it goes on a black box 'technical roadmap'." — Context: from "One Roadmap," on how critical infrastructure work becomes invisible when split off. Source: "TBM 48/53: One Roadmap," <https://cutlefish.substack.com/p/tbm-4853-one-roadmap> (fetched). The same piece's recommendation, also verbatim: "So…as a healthy forcing function…try to keep it all on one roadmap." (ellipses are Cutler's own, preserved).

**What he attacks in a product artifact.**

- "Are you installing the artifacts of a mature operating model, or building the conditions (judgment, learning, constraint) that produce one?" — means–ends inversion / cargo-culting a framework.
- "Where's the coherence as this adapts — or is 'the model' a frozen org chart?" — treating the operating model as a one-time destination.
- "Is the foundational/infra work on the same visible roadmap as feature work, or hidden in a technical black box?" — governance that structurally devalues enabling work.
- "Who is ProductOps' customer here — cross-functional teams, or leadership reporting?" — a process/ops function that serves dashboards instead of teams (his stated "big red flag").
- "What evidence will tell you this operating change is working, and how will you keep adapting it?" — static process with no feedback or learning loop.

**Sourcing caveat.** All five quotes were fetched from Cutler's own Substack (primary), and care was taken to flag where segments are non-adjacent (quotes 2, 3, and the two halves of quote 4) so nothing reads as a single fabricated sentence. The Substack note (quotes 1–3) is dated 2024/25 and is a short-form "Note," so cite it as a note, not a full essay. His current title (Head of Product, Dotwork) is corroborated by LinkedIn and a 2025 conference listing. The em-dash in "means–ends" and the ellipses in the "One Roadmap" quote are his own punctuation, preserved verbatim.
