# Category Design — designing the container, not competing in it

The most ambitious move in `02-positioning`: rather than win share inside an existing category, **design and name a new one** the brand can be king of. Products don't live in space — they live in a *container in the customer's mind*, and that container is a category. The economics are winner-take-most: the category king commands the lion's share of the category's value, and the king is usually the company that *designed and named the category*, not the one with the best product.

## The method

1. **Design the problem, not just the product.** Make a half-felt pain legible — name it — so your product becomes the obvious answer. The unit of work is a **point of view**: a story that frames a problem, spells out the cost of not acting, paints the after-world, and only then arrives at your solution.
2. **Different, not better.** Competing on *better* keeps you inside the incumbent's frame and yardstick (the loser's game). Category kings change the question. The discipline is "frame it, name it, claim it" — give the new way language the market can adopt.
3. **Condition the market.** A category isn't real until others use its words. Mobilize a concentrated push (the "lightning strike") that moves minds from the old way to the new, and recruit the obsessed early cohort whose latent demand makes the category believable.

## What good looks like

The market adopts *your words without you in the room*: analysts draw a quadrant, the problem becomes a budget line, competitors validate the category by entering it — and in the strongest cases **a new job title appears** (the surest sign a category is real, because it exists independent of any one vendor). A genuinely new category also unlocks pricing power: customers accept a price the old category never would.

## The hard test

**Does anyone but you use the words?** Sharpest first: (1) if only the coining company and its paid channels say it, it's copy, not a category; (2) does it reframe a problem people *already feel*; (3) strongest — has it changed behavior *outside* the product (a budget line, an analyst category, a conference, a job title)? If you can only describe the offering as a modifier of an existing category ("the premium / AI version of X"), you are competing, not designing.

## The anti-pattern

**The press-release category** — declaring a category nobody believes in. A new noun and a launch post cannot will a category into existence; if the words don't change how people think about the problem, you've added jargon to a fight you don't control. Adjacent failures: the **category of one** with zero adopters (a soliloquy — a real category exerts gravitational pull, pulling even rivals in), and **renaming an existing category** and calling it new (the "better trap" wearing a synonym). Category design is the right move only when there is a real, felt, mis-framed problem and the will to evangelize the category itself — otherwise, own a territory inside the existing one (`positioning-territories.md`).
