# The Transformation Story — who the customer becomes

The `01-foundation` component that names the change a brand promises: not what the product *does*, but *who the customer becomes* by using it. The single greatest human motivation is the desire for transformation, so the deepest brands sell an identity shift, not a feature. This reference is the method for stating that before → after; it descends from the position and feeds the voice (`05-voice`) and product (`06-product`).

## The method

1. **Cast the customer as the hero, the brand as the guide.** The customer is the protagonist who *wants* something and faces a problem; the brand is the mentor who supplies the plan and the tools. The guide carries two traits — **empathy** ("I understand your pain") and **authority** ("I've helped others win"). The brand never owns the journey.
2. **Name the problem on three levels.** The **external** obstacle (the practical thing in the way), the **internal** feeling it creates (frustration, doubt, fear), and the **philosophical** stake (why it's *wrong* that the customer should suffer it). Feature claims answer only the external; transformation answers all three.
3. **State the identity shift as a pair.** Write *"Before: the customer was ___; After: they became ___,"* where both blanks are an **identity or status**, not a feature or a number. "From a confused beginner → a confident host," not "from slow reporting → fast reporting." Reach the social job — *who the customer wants to be seen as* — not just the functional one.

## What good looks like

A nameable before-identity → after-identity the customer would *say about themselves*. It escalates through the three problem levels and casts the brand as guide throughout. At its most ambitious, the after-identity resolves a *cultural* tension, not just a personal task — the brand performs an identity myth the audience borrows to become someone.

## The hard test

Three, sharpest first: (1) **Name the two identities** — who the customer *was* and *becomes*, each an identity, not a feature gain. If the only honest "after" is "saved time," it's benefit-clarity, not a transformation. (2) **Check the casting** — is the brand the *guide*, not the hero? The protagonist of every sentence should be the customer. (3) For ambitious brands — does the after-identity resolve a tension bigger than the task?

## The anti-pattern

**Brand-as-hero** — the company casts *itself* as the protagonist that swoops in and saves the day ("we're the experts, we're #1"); the customer has no role and tunes out. Adjacent failures: **feature-dumping** (a spec list with no person in it), **"we're the best"** superlatives standing in for a stated after-identity, and **aspiration with no circumstance** ("become your best self" with no named situation and no plan). If the customer isn't the hero of the sentence, there is no transformation story.
