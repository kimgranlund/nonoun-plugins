# The Editorial Style Guide — governing voice as a publication's house style

The `05-voice` expansion: governing tone, naming, and copy as an *editorial discipline*, the way a publication governs its house style. The point of a style guide is that an outsider could write from it and produce on-brand copy **without the founder in the room**. This reference is the method for building one; it descends from the point of view in `01-foundation` (voice is the POV spoken aloud).

## The method

1. **Separate voice from tone.** **Voice** is the stable personality — the same on every surface. **Tone** is its modulation, and it shifts with *the reader's emotional state*: an error screen and a launch announcement need different tones in the same voice. Conflating the two is the first failure; "be funny" is a tone the reader's state must earn, not a constant.
2. **Define each voice attribute as a boundary, not an adjective.** Use the *"we are X but not Y" / "we prefer X to Y"* construction — "dry but not flippant," "we prefer winking to shouting." The boundary is what operationalizes the attribute; a bare adjective constrains nothing.
3. **Map tone to context by the reader's emotional state.** Organize by content type — success, error, onboarding, legal, outage — each keyed to how the reader likely feels, with worked before/after examples. Standing rule: it is always more important to be *clear* than entertaining; forced humor is worse than none.
4. **Make the rules concrete.** A populated **do / don't** table of real on-brand vs off-brand phrasings; a **words-we-use / words-we-avoid** list; grammar and mechanics as house style (active voice, plain words, cut slack — "if you can cut a word, cut it"). Treat the brand literally like a publication.

## What good looks like

A guide a stranger could write on-brand copy from, cold: voice attributes with bounded definitions, tone mapped to named contexts with examples, a populated do/don't table, an explicit words-to-avoid list, and grammar rules. Crucially, it contains **specific refusals** — words the brand will never use, jokes it won't make.

## The hard test

Three, sharpest first: (1) **Does the guide tell you what NOT to say?** (2) **Could two different writers produce consistent copy from it, cold?** (3) **Does the voice make refusals** — things it will never do or say? A voice with no refusals isn't a voice; it's a vibe.

## The anti-pattern

**A voice "for everyone"** with no refusals — it can say anything, so it says nothing. Adjacent failures: **adjective soup** ("friendly, professional, innovative, trustworthy," which three rivals also list and none of which constrains a sentence — the fix is "but not" + concrete pairs); **tone that never varies by context** (campaign cheer on an error message, legalese on a success state); and **aesthetic-led, not authority-led** — codifying how copy *looks* without the point of view that earns the right to speak that way.
