# Positioning Territories — staking ground you can own

The `02-positioning` expansion of the position decided in `01-foundation`. A **territory** is the ground a brand chooses to own in the customer's mind — and, just as load-bearingly, the ground it gives up. Positioning is done to the *mind of the prospect*, not the product: you win an empty slot, not an argument. This reference is the method for generating and testing candidate territories; the chosen one feeds back into the foundation's position.

## The method

1. **Start from the real alternative, not the assumed competitor.** What does the customer do if you don't exist? Often that's a spreadsheet, an incumbent, or *nothing* — not the rival you'd name. The frame of reference you pick decides everything downstream: call yourself a CRM and you're judged against the category king; reframe and you're judged on your own terms.
2. **Find the empty slot.** The mind holds one slot per idea, already occupied by whoever got there first. You either take an unowned slot or reframe the category so a new slot opens. Being *first in the mind* beats being first to market.
3. **Reduce to one idea.** The strongest territory is reducible to a single word or a single "only" clause. Generate candidates, then compress each: *"the only [category] that [point of radical difference], for [who], unlike [the alternative]."*
4. **Balance parity and difference.** Claim *just enough* table-stakes parity to be considered in the category, then spend everything else on one sharp difference. All-parity is invisible; all-difference with no parity is unbelievable.

## What good looks like

A territory that is **true** (grounded in a real capability, not an aspiration), **narrow enough to be first**, **single-minded** (one word, one clause), and **credible** (backed by proof a rival can't borrow). The tell is behavioral: best-fit customers "get it" fast and buy quickly; weak positioning shows as long sales cycles, price pressure, and "our customers love us but new prospects don't understand what we sell."

## The hard test

**The competitor-substitution test.** Write the onliness clause, then swap in your three strongest rivals' names. If it stays true for even one of them, it is not a territory — it's a category description. A real territory *breaks* the instant a competitor's name is inserted. (This is the `brand-evaluate` strategy rubric's exclusivity bar, applied at authoring time.)

## The anti-pattern

**The "of course" trap** — a territory any competitor could stand behind without blinking: "customer-obsessed," "innovative," "premium quality," "trusted," "data-driven." Each is true *of you* and equally true of everyone, so it installs no slot. If the clause isn't an "only," it's a "me-too." The fix is sacrifice: name the adjacent ground — and the customer — you are deliberately giving up. A position that gives up nothing claims nothing.
