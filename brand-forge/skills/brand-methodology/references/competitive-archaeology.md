# Competitive Archaeology — reading the category's codes before you break them

The `02-positioning` work of excavating a category as a *system of meaning*: the conventions, codes, and history every player obeys, so you know what to honor, what to subvert, and what to abandon. A category is a grammar of recognition — break the wrong code and you become illegible; break none and you become invisible.

## The method

1. **Sort the codes by their place in time.** Audit the category's signs into three layers: **residual** (the once-dominant, now-fading conventions — heritage or staleness), **dominant** (today's markers everyone obeys — *the rules of the category*), and **emergent** (new cues converging into tomorrow's themes). The triad tells you which codes are load-bearing and which are decaying.
2. **Decide honor / subvert / abandon.** **Honor** the *category entry codes* — the dominant codes you must obey to be understood (a beer must read as beer; a water must read as a drink). **Subvert** the codes that have curdled into clichés of sameness — break the *right* dominant code for contrast. **Abandon** the residual/decayed codes, and for a challenger, the dominant set entirely — borrowing an *adjacent* category's codes instead.
3. **Inventory who owns which cue.** Map the category's distinctive assets on two axes — **fame** (how many link the cue to a brand) × **uniqueness** (how exclusively it links to *one* brand). The danger zone is *high-fame, low-uniqueness*: a cue famous but category-generic ("that kind of brand") that you cannot own and that invites copying. Invest in the high-uniqueness cues that are genuinely yours.

## What good looks like

A brand that obeys *enough* dominant codes to be found in-category, breaks the *right* one for contrast, and owns at least one high-fame / high-uniqueness asset that triggers recognition with the logo removed. The reliable signal that a design decision is wrong is that *everyone in the category is already making it* — when you identify a norm, resist it, but selectively: break the cliché, keep the entry code.

## The hard test

**The double recognition test:** cover the name and blur the logo — (1) is it still recognizable as *itself*? **and** (2) does it still read as a member of its category? A brand can be unmistakably itself yet category-illegible (broke too many entry codes), or category-legible yet interchangeable (broke none). Passing *both* is the bar.

## The anti-pattern

**The sea of sameness** — copying dominant codes so faithfully the brand lands in the high-fame/low-uniqueness zone (or generic low/low). The cue points at the *category*, not the brand. A category where everyone makes the same choices has lost the will to differentiate; the archaeology exists precisely to find the one code worth breaking and the one worth keeping.
