# The Brand Foundation Canon

The foundation is the **load-bearing** layer of a brand — the small set of decisions everything
expressive descends from. This reference defines what a foundation must contain, what it must
reject, and how short it is allowed to be.

> The test for any foundation document: **could a competitor sign their name to it?** If yes,
> it is not a foundation. It is a category description with your logo on top.

---

## The six load-bearing components

A foundation is complete when it answers these six. Each must be written down, each must be
*specific to this brand*, and each must be defensible from a primary source — not a moodboard.

### 1. The cultural root
The real world this brand comes from — its history, ritual, originators, and lived practice.
Not "the running market"; *running* — the suffering, the dawn miles, the people who were doing
it before it was a category. Trace it to primary sources: archives, fieldwork, the actual people.
**Failure mode:** the root is a survey of five competitors. That is a landscape, not a root.

### 2. The position
The single space this brand owns in the customer's mind, stated so it **excludes**. A position
that fits any competitor is not a position. Name the frame, the alternative it beats, and — load
this bit explicitly — **who it is not for**.
**Test:** can you complete "the only brand that ___, for people who ___, *unlike* ___"?

### 3. The point of view
What this brand *believes* about its world that others won't say out loud. A POV is an argument,
not an adjective. "We believe X, and therefore we refuse Y." It is the source of everything the
brand says; voice is just the POV, spoken.
**Failure mode:** a POV that everyone in the category already shares. Then it is table stakes,
not a view.

### 4. The enemy / the tension
The thing the brand stands *against* — a complacent incumbent, a lazy convention, a cultural
falsehood. Brands with no enemy have no energy. The enemy need not be a competitor; often it is
an attitude. This is what makes a position *cost* something.
**Test:** name what the brand will lose by holding this line. If nothing, there is no tension.

### 5. The customer transformation
**Who does the customer become** by choosing this brand? Brand is identity borrowed. Describe the
before-self and the after-self — the status, capability, or belonging the customer is buying.
This is the actual product; the object is the receipt.
**Failure mode:** describing the customer (a *persona*) instead of their transformation. See the
filter below.

### 6. The editorial principles
The small set of rules — three to seven — that decide what is "us" and what gets cut. Written as
**we do / we don't**, with trade-offs. "We write plainly even when grand language would sell
faster." These principles let anyone extend the brand without asking permission.
**Test:** every principle must forbid something you would otherwise be tempted to do.

---

## The bullshit filter

The Canon actively **rejects** four artifacts that feel like strategy but commit to nothing. They
are popular because they are easy, look thorough, and require no decisions. Reject them by name —
and build the load-bearing component instead.

| Rejected artifact | Why it fails | Build this instead |
|---|---|---|
| **Brand archetypes** ("we're the Hero / Sage / Outlaw") | A 12-slot horoscope. Generic by construction; any brand can claim any archetype. Replaces a *decided* position with a *borrowed* one. | The **position** (§2) and **point of view** (§3) — specific, exclusive, argued. |
| **Vision / Mission / Values triplets** | Interchangeable corporate boilerplate. "Values" with no trade-off are wishes; missions are usually the category's mission. | **Editorial principles with trade-offs** (§6) and the **enemy** (§4). |
| **Personas** ("meet Sarah, 34, loves brunch") | Describe a *demographic snapshot*, not what the customer is trying to *become*. Optimizes for targeting, not meaning. | The **customer transformation** (§5) — before-self → after-self. |
| **"Brand DNA" word-clouds** ("authentic · bold · human · premium") | Adjectives asserted, never earned. Every brand picks the same six words. Tells instead of shows. | The **cultural root** (§1) — concrete artifacts and primary sources that *demonstrate* the adjective. |

The unifying defect: each artifact lets a team feel finished without ever **deciding** or
**excluding** anything. The filter's job is to convert "we feel premium" into "we refuse to
discount, and here is what that costs us."

---

## The 3-page minimum-viable foundation

A real foundation fits on **three pages**:

1. **The root & the enemy** — where we come from, what we stand against.
2. **The position & POV** — the space we own, the belief that earns it, who it is not for.
3. **The transformation & the principles** — who the customer becomes, and the we-do/we-don't
   rules that keep us honest.

If it cannot be said in three pages, the thinking is not done. Length is usually a symptom: extra
pages hide an undecided position behind research dumps, mood imagery, and adjective lists. Write
the three pages first; the deck, the guidelines, and the corpus are *expansions* of them, never
substitutes.

> **Extension point.** A mature brand corpus expands each component into its own reference —
> positioning territories, category-design narrative, competitive archaeology, the transformation
> story, the editorial style guide. They all descend from these three pages. If they ever
> contradict the three pages, the three pages win.
